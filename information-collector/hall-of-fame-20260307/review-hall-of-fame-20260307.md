### 原文全文（完整问答）

**On Life After Olympic Gold**

**Interviewer:** "Are you staying here?"
**Garnbret:** "Yes!" she replies shyly, with a look of both disbelief and amusement.

When asked about drinks, "Garnbret goes all-out and asks for an orange juice."

"My personal life hasn't changed, I'm still the same as I was 10 years ago!" she laughs.

**On Childhood and Natural Ability**

**Garnbret:** "I was a really hyperactive child. I just wanted to do everything. I was climbing on doorframes and trees — everywhere!"

Regarding her parents' athleticism: "They were active but not pro athletes, so I don't know how I came out... like this!" she laughs.

**On Her Climbing Style Evolution**

**Garnbret:** "Over the years, my climbing style changed a lot. When I came onto the scene in 2015, I was more of a static and slower climber as I was focusing mostly on Lead. In 2017, I started competing in bouldering and something clicked. I became more dynamic."

On learning dynamic moves: "I guess I had it in me, I guess that was just something fun for me. I didn't need much time to learn. I guess it came naturally to me."

**On Competitive Pressure**

**Garnbret:** "I won there, so ever since I feel like when I'm competing, I have to win. I have to perform my best, I shouldn't have a bad day."

On external expectations: "That's not necessarily a pressure, as I try to take it as a positive that people believe that I'm capable of winning every competition."

**On Mental Challenges**

**Garnbret:** "Most of the pressure actually comes from me because I have high expectations to perform at my best at every competition, but also at every training session."

On perfectionism: "I want everything to be perfect, I want to be better than I was yesterday and than I was in previous years. I want to improve this and that, I just want to be the best overall climber possible."

On balancing perfectionism: "Perfectionism can slow your progress because sometimes, you should be more gentle on yourself and not push yourself too much. Sometimes I don't know how to do that because I put too much pressure on myself."

**On Her Coach Roman Krajnik**

**Garnbret:** "We started working together at the end of 2019, but it felt like we'd been doing it for 15 years. We both have the same energy. He pours his heart out for training, and so do I, so those two energies can create something magical."

On areas for improvement: "I'm not so good at slabs so we've been working a lot on that, and movement and route climbing — on everything!"

On training with teammates: "We have so many strong women who can compete for podium places. So you get a comparison at nationals or team training and it's great when we push each other."

**On 2019 Setbacks**

**Garnbret:** "I feel like you always learn more from your failures than your wins."

On the Chamonix competition: "Maybe it just wasn't my day, or maybe since 2019 was the season when I won all Boulder World Cups then I won the first Lead World Cup, I felt like I could win every single competition that season."

On home pressure in Kranj: "It's always hard to compete in Kranj because you always want to perform your best and people expect you to, so I learned that you can have a bad day."

**Interviewer:** "...and that you're human?"
**Garnbret:** "Yeah!" she laughs.

**On Tokyo 2020 Preparation**

**Garnbret:** "It was insane. If I ever said that I was under pressure in a World Cup or World Championships, the Olympics were a different story."

On avoiding media: "Number one thing: I didn't read anything. Before the Olympics, I didn't read any magazine, any single news report — nothing."

On the Olympic Village experience: "We were in the Olympic Village three days before our competition and everybody was walking around with medals and celebrating and I hadn't even started competing yet. That was tough mentally."

**On Speed Qualifiers**

**Garnbret:** "I was crying so hard after my two speed runs. I said to Roman: 'I trained for two years to get 10 seconds on the Speed run!' I was pissed and frustrated with myself."

On the Boulder performance that followed: "That was probably because of my frustration and anger — I showed that in the Boulder round."

**On the Finals**

**Garnbret:** "When I woke up in the morning I felt different, I felt in the zone already and I was just ready to go all-in. If you ask me what I was thinking when I was competing in the finals, I wouldn't know because I was in my own bubble, in my own world and I don't remember a thing!"

On mental fatigue: "I was super tired from Speed and Boulder, but not physically, more mentally because the competition lasts so long."

On her approach before the lead route: "Before my lead route, I was listening to music and thinking 'Just this one last route and then you're done!' I just wanted to be done with the Olympics. My mind was at peace. I knew what I had to do. I said 'You need to have fun!'. I'm always repeating that, but I know that I give my best performance when I'm having fun."

**On the Lead Route Performance**

**Garnbret:** "I had no idea where I was placed, I was just trying to get as high as possible."

On the route difficulty: "If that same route was in a World Cup final, we would have different climbing. The route was top-able, that's for sure. If it was a World Cup, the pressure would be different."

On her fall: "When I fell I remember that I was happy — I knew I gave my best as I was tired."

**On Winning**

**Garnbret:** "When Miho fell I knew that I had won, but I couldn't really realise that I had actually done it because Chaehyun Seo was still climbing after Miho. I was sitting there waiting and then I started crying because I actually realised that I won."

On the podium: "I think this was the best podium ever, Akiyo ending her career with a medal and Miho was also injured before the Olympics. It was so great for us to be together on the podium."

On the medal itself: "It felt surreal. The medal weighs half a kilo. So it's a proper medal and it's really amazing to have it!"

**On Handling Olympic Pressure**

**Garnbret:** "Every time I reflect on the last year, I find it crazy that I actually won the competition, because we all know stories of athletes winning every single World Cup before the Olympics and then at the Olympics... stuff happened! I find it even more fascinating that I actually handled the pressure."

On the no-spectator environment: "I could take it as just another training competition. Of course with spectators it would have been even more amazing because the atmosphere would have been great, but without a crowd I felt like I could be more focused and just do my thing."

**On the Film "The Wall: Climb for Gold"**

**Garnbret:** "I really enjoyed watching the film because every everybody knows each other, but nobody knows what's behind everything and it's not always as it seems. We were all working towards the Tokyo Olympics, but we had very different stories: Miho was injured just before the Olympics, Shauna was dealing with injuries and she was at the end of her career, Brooke is just starting her career and I _had_ to win, basically."

On competition climbing representation: "We have many rock climbing videos, but we have never had a movie dedicated to competitions. With the Olympics, it was the best opportunity to do it."

On watching the film: "This is the seventh time I've watched the film and I still get emotional!"

**On Impact in Slovenia**

**Garnbret:** "Everybody knows about climbing now. 10 years ago, when I said that I climb, people said 'Oh, is that like alpinism? What even is that?' Now everybody knows the difference between all three disciplines and all the rules. All the clubs are full and there's a waiting list of 150 kids just to join a club."

**On Post-Olympic Competition Return**

**Garnbret:** "I was super emotional in Meiringen, and I was also surprised. I don't take any wins for granted and also I try not to compare my accomplishments to the previous season because if you win once, it doesn't mean you will win in the next season because new competitors arrive and anything can happen. I'm super grateful that I can still be the best."

**On Future Plans**

**Garnbret:** "I need a break from competitions because the Olympics really did take a toll on my mental and physical health. But I also have some rock climbing projects and this is a perfect time to try those."

On "La Dura Dura" outdoor project: "It is hard — hard hard! I hope that it will go. I did all the moves. One move in the middle is a bit problematic. It's super reachy. In the middle of the route, where Chris [Sharma] and Adam [Ondra] jump, I have to jump jump! I'm looking for a challenge and I feel like La Dura Dura is the perfect one for me."

On Paris 2024: "I always have Paris in mind and it's really important to pick the right competitions because I want to be in Paris 100% focused and motivated. I don't want to be in Paris tired from the previous seasons. Even though it seems far away now, it will be here fast!"

**On Lead Climbing Potential**

**Garnbret:** "I feel like I haven't reached my potential in Lead yet. I was always good in Lead but I've never had the feeling I have in bouldering of being super, super confident."

On younger competitors: "Youngsters have to come. It feels weird, because some years ago I was 15 and just coming up and Shauna was 24 and now it's the other way around. It's super fun to see that they can keep up. It feels great to get a slap in the face sometimes by youngsters!"

**On Future Achievement**

**Garnbret:** "No! I still haven't won a European Championships. And yeah, I'm not done yet! I still find motivation in that. I can't be better than first, but I have a lot of potential to work on my mental training. I feel like I'm not at my peak yet. I can still improve in all aspects of climbing. I don't think I will ever get bored of competitions!"

### 原文全文

**Overview**

Slovenia's Janja Garnbret, the most successful competitive climber in history, discusses her motivations, training regimen, and future plans. Her achievements include an Olympic gold medal, six World Championship titles, four European championships, and 37 IFSC World Cup victories.

**On Motivation and Perfectionism**

"Every day is another fight. I'm a perfectionist and I want to be great."

Rather than resting on her accolades, Garnbret treats every tournament round as a separate challenge requiring maximum effort. She believes starting fresh daily enables her to maintain competitive edge against equally dedicated opponents.

**On Training Regimen**

During typical training seasons (November through April), Garnbret commits substantial time to her craft:

- **Six training sessions weekly** lasting four to five hours each
- **95 percent of training occurs on the wall** rather than supplementary fitness work
- Additional physiotherapy sessions focusing on injury prevention (approximately 90 minutes)
- Increased volume during Olympic preparation years

**On Progress as Addiction**

"For me, it's always been progress. It's addictive, because sometimes in training a hold feels very bad, but then two months later this hold feels very good. So, it's the progress that is addictive, because you see that training is working and you get stronger and stronger. I'm addicted to progress."

**On Handling Pressure**

Despite her dominance, Garnbret acknowledges experiencing self-doubt and media pressure. Her coping strategy involves remaining present-focused rather than anticipating outcomes. She explains that supportive crowds help dispel uncertainty, allowing her to perform optimally.

**On Mentorship**

Garnbret views her role as a mentor to younger climbers, particularly girls. Having admired previous climber Mia Krampl, she now occupies that inspirational position. She emphasizes demonstrating that athletic pursuits need not involve suffering and that dedication yields achievable goals.

**On Future**

"I will end my career when I stop having fun."

Rather than contemplating retirement, Garnbret continues seeking fresh challenges. She intends competing as long as enjoyment persists, subsequently pursuing outdoor climbing and coaching younger athletes.

**Training Schedule**

Adam's typical training week during hard training periods:
- **Monday–Thursday**: Hard training days
- **Friday**: Rest day
- **Saturday–Sunday**: Outdoor climbing (weather permitting)

**Campus Board**

"My campus board session would never really take more than forty minutes, plus some warmup. In total maybe it is one hour and ten."

Adam performs campus boarding with focused explosiveness. Sessions never exceed 40 minutes of actual work.

**Endurance Circuit Training**

Eight rounds of 50-move circuits with two-minute rest intervals, completed in approximately 40 minutes.

**Interval Training ("4x4x4")**

Four boulder problems repeated four times with short rest, then longer rest, repeated four times total.

**Calf Training for Kneebars**

Adam performed interval training standing on toes with knees at 90 degrees, holding 40 seconds with short rest periods. This training tripled his kneebar hanging duration.

**On Finger Training**

Adam avoids traditional fingerboarding due to skin damage concerns but trains finger strength through bouldering.

**Rest and Recovery**

- Annual break: 2–3 weeks in December
- Multiple short sessions daily rather than one long session
- Adequate rest between sessions allows faster recovery

### 原文全文

Adam Ondra was born February 5, 1993, in Brno, Czech Republic. His parents were climbers who introduced him to the sport at age six. By 1999, at just six years old, he climbed a 6a route in Rovinj, Croatia. His rapid progression became legendary—he onsighted 7b+ routes by age eight and achieved his first graded 9a redpoint at thirteen.

**Record-Breaking Accomplishments:**
- First climber to redpoint a 9c (5.15d) route: *Silence* (2017)
- First to redpoint a 9b+ (5.15c): *Change* (2012)
- First to flash a 9a+ (5.15a): *Super Crackinette* (2018)
- Only male athlete to win World Championship titles in both lead climbing and bouldering in a single year (2014)

**Competition Success:**

Ondra won World Cup titles in both lead climbing (2009, 2015, 2019) and bouldering (2010). He competed in the 2020 and 2024 Olympics, finishing sixth in the combined events both times.

**The Silence Achievement:**

On September 3, 2017, Ondra completed *Silence* after approximately four years of dedicated work. This route represents the world's first proposed 9c grade—the highest difficulty level in sport climbing. Located in Hanshelleren Cave, Flatanger Municipality, Norway.

**Route Statistics:**

By November 2018, Ondra had completed 1,550+ routes graded 8a or harder, including: one 9c, four 9b+, twenty 9b routes, and numerous 9a ascents.

**Physical Profile:** Height 186 cm, Weight 70 kg, Ape Index +1 cm.

### 原文全文

Speed climbing is a climbing discipline in which speed is the ultimate goal. Competition speed climbing takes place on 15-meter artificial walls with a 5-degree overhang. Competitors race side-by-side on identical routes. Modern competitions use mechanical-electric timing accurate to one-thousandth of a second.

**Men's Record Progression:**

Starting from 6.26 seconds in 2011 (Zhong Qixin, China), the record dropped to 4.64 seconds by May 2025 (Samuel Watson, USA)—a 25.9% reduction.

**Women's Record Progression:**

From 7.85 seconds (2013) to 6.03 seconds (September 2025, Aleksandra Mirosław). Mirosław has held the world record since 2021, breaking it 10+ times.

**Notable Athletes:**
- **Samuel Watson (USA)**: Current men's world record holder (4.64s, 2025)
- **Aleksandra Mirosław (Poland)**: Dominant women's record holder
- **Veddriq Leonardo (Indonesia)**: Paris 2024 Olympic gold medalist, broke the 5-second barrier
- **Kiromal Katibin (Indonesia)**: Held men's world record through 2022

**Olympic Integration:**

Speed climbing was included in the combined format at Tokyo 2020. From Paris 2024, it became a standalone Olympic event.

### 原文全文

Alexander J Honnold was born August 17, 1985, in Sacramento, California. He began climbing at age 5 in a gym. After graduating high school in 2003, he enrolled at UC Berkeley studying civil engineering but dropped out to pursue climbing full-time, initially living in a van.

**Historic El Capitan Achievement:**

On June 3, 2017, Honnold became the first person to free solo the complete Freerider route on El Capitan in Yosemite National Park. The 884-meter (2,900-foot) climb at grade 5.13a took 3 hours and 56 minutes. The New York Times described this as "one of the great athletic feats of any kind, ever."

The climb was documented in the 2018 documentary *Free Solo*, which won the Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature.

**Climbing Philosophy:**

"I was never, like, a bad climber [as a kid], but I had never been a great climber, either... I just loved climbing, and I've been climbing all the time ever since, so I've naturally gotten better at it."

**Recent Achievement:**

On January 25, 2026, Honnold completed the first-ever free solo ascent of Taipei 101 in Taiwan, reaching the 508-meter structure's summit in 1 hour and 36 minutes without ropes.

Tommy Caldwell was born August 11, 1978, in Estes Park, Colorado. His father, Mike Caldwell, was a mountain guide and rock climber who introduced Tommy to climbing at a young age. The family made annual trips to Yosemite National Park, where Caldwell's passion for climbing flourished.

**Major Achievements:**

*Flex Luthor* (9a+/5.15a) in 2003 at Colorado's Fortress of Solitude—remained unrepeated for 18 years. First free ascents of multiple El Capitan routes including *Dihedral Wall* and *West Buttress*.

**The Dawn Wall (2015):**

In January 2015, Caldwell and Kevin Jorgeson completed the first free climb of The Dawn Wall on El Capitan over 19 days—"the first-ever big wall free climb at 9a (5.14d) in history." President Barack Obama congratulated them: "You remind us that anything is possible."

**Speed Record:**

In 2018, Caldwell and Alex Honnold set a sub-2-hour speed record on The Nose (1:58:07).

**Accolade:**

National Geographic called Caldwell "arguably the best all-around rock climber on the planet" in 2015.

**Overcoming Adversity:**

Despite losing most of his left index finger in a 2001 table saw accident, Caldwell continued climbing at elite levels. He also survived a six-day hostage situation in Kyrgyzstan in 2000.

### 原文全文

In September 1993, Lynn Hill accomplished a groundbreaking achievement when she completed the first free ascent of The Nose on El Capitan. Partnered with Brooke Sandahl, the 32-year-old climber took four days in mid-September. This ascent "stunned absolutely everyone" at the time.

Hill's journey to this achievement included an earlier 1989 attempt with British climber Simon Nadin, during which they unlocked one of the route's most challenging sections: "the famous Great Roof."

**The One-Day Free Ascent (1994):**

In September 1994, Hill and Steve Sutton completed a free ascent in just 23 hours, starting at night on September 19. Described as "lightyears ahead of its time" and "a major milestone in the rich history of rock climbing."

**Route Specifications:**

The Nose spans 870 meters across 31 pitches, grade 5.13c. First ascended in 1958 by Warren Harding, Wayne Merry, and George Whitmore (47 days).

**Legacy:**

As of 2018, free repeats of The Nose remained exceptionally rare, with only a handful of climbers successfully completing free ascents in 25 years.
