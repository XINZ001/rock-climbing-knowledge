### 原文全文

**Climbing:** The last time we talked, it was around this time after the Tokyo Olympics and I asked you how you were celebrating. You said you just wanted to go shopping for a pair of jeans with friends. How are you celebrating now—has there been any purchase bigger than a pair of jeans?

**Garnbret:** "Not yet. I was mostly celebrating—they made a huge reception in my hometown—with the people that supported me since I was very little."

---

**Climbing:** Another thing you said three years ago was that you didn't know where you were going to put the Tokyo Olympics gold medal. I'm guessing the Paris one is going to go next to the Tokyo one.

**Garnbret:** "It's going next to the Tokyo one. Right now, I'm building a house, so I need to find a good space for both of them."

---

**Climbing:** Adidas TERREX released the documentary series *Janja Garnbret Unfiltered* during the Paris Olympics, and in episode 2, you said that you didn't feel the Paris Olympics pressure yet—but you knew a time would come when you *would.* When did you start feeling the pressure of the Paris Olympics, and what was that like?

**Garnbret:** "I think three months before the Tokyo Olympics, I already felt the pressure building up every single day. It was really tough—it felt like a burden."

She explained that before Paris, she felt less pressure because she already had one gold medal and more Olympic experience. "I'd already had one Olympic experience. Plus, in those three years since Tokyo, I learned a lot about myself, and about training."

"The first time that I felt pressure was in the finals. I wasn't stressed in semis—it all went smooth; I really felt like I was super focused and never in my life have I believed in myself like I did in Paris. Not a single moment of doubt."

Garnbret described her mindset shift: "I felt like I was a different person in Paris compared to Tokyo—of course, I was the same one, but the mentality was different."

---

**Climbing:** That's interesting because I was wondering what your mindset was in between the semi-finals and the finals in Paris. You had a fantastic performance in the semi-finals: You flashed the first two boulders; the third boulder took you two attempts; the fourth boulder took you four attempts; and you fell reaching for the top hold on the lead route. After all that, were you trying to just mentally erase everything that just happened—because everybody starts over in the finals? Or were you somehow trying to take a confidence boost from the semis and carry that into the finals?

**Garnbret:** "I kind of felt like I proved myself in the bouldering. Then I proved to myself that I was also ready in lead."

She emphasized the importance of balance: "Because I'm a perfectionist, you always want to give, every time, a little bit more. But I learned for the Olympics it's important that you don't do too little [and] you don't do too much."

Her strategy was straightforward: "Just do what you do in training, or like you do at every single World Cup." She added, "Just be calm. Don't panic. Don't be frustrated. Just do your thing—and even if something comes up in between, like the potential finger injury, don't let it affect you."

---

**Climbing:** What happened with that finger injury on the fourth boulder in finals? It seemed like it maybe got stuck in the hold, and I remember watching and wondering how bad it was.

**Garnbret:** "Yeah, it was the fourth boulder. The starting hold was actually blocked on the left side. And my fingers got kind of under the blocked hold, and when I was about to jump, my finger didn't come off."

She recalled her fear: "I was really scared at that moment because I could easily break a finger in that situation." However, she was able to continue. "I have to admit that I was crying quite hard after that boulder. But then my physio checked the finger. He said, 'It's not broken and the ligaments look fine.'"

Despite the pain, she remained determined: "Even if I climb without one finger, I will still go and climb that route because it's the Olympics, the Olympics are every four years."

---

**Climbing:** I would imagine after a finger injury like that, your emotions are high—you said you were crying—and there is probably an aspect of needing to get back to baseline. What do you do in that situation? How do you handle that?

**Garnbret:** "Honestly, I have no idea. I have no idea how I handled it."

She focused on what she knew: "When Roman [Garnbret's coach] and I went to warm up, I knew that my finger was not seriously injured, so I knew I could continue."

Her self-talk was powerful: "This is your chance; this is your day. Whatever happens, it will be OK."

She noted that competition adrenaline helped: "When you're competing, adrenaline is there, so your mind is not on your finger but more on what's coming up next."

Before the lead route, she told herself: "You've got this…just do it like you do it in training." She reflected, "I just climbed—I can say I *enjoyed* it. I didn't want the pressure to take the fun out of competing."

Her deeper motivation remained clear: "I needed to remember why I started, and why I was doing this—it's always because I love climbing, that's why I do it."

---

**Climbing:** At the end of that lead route, when you were lowering down, it was an incredible moment. We saw the emotion come over you, and the first person to run over and congratulate you was Brooke Raboutou. That was interesting because it was also the person that you had just beaten for the gold medal, so I think it was a wonderful moment of camaraderie. Do you remember what was spoken between the two of you in that moment?

**Garnbret:** "Me and Brooke have a special relationship. Of course, we share a passion for climbing, but we share, also, the same mentality on life and on training."

The two climbers had hoped to share the podium: "I think it was kind of our little wish, our little secret, to be on the podium together. We wanted it to happen in Tokyo, but it didn't work out. So for Paris, we really wanted to be on the podium together."

Garnbret trained with Raboutou in February and remembered her feelings at the time: "Already in February I said, 'This feels like a podium to me.' I think I said, 'If someone deserves to be on the podium, it's you,' and 'We did it, together.'"

She called it her favorite Olympic moment: "It was probably my favorite moment from the Olympics, that moment with Brooke."

When reflecting on the moment later, she appreciated Raboutou's genuine support: "When I rewatched the finals later, I could see that Brooke was really happy for me—even if I beat her, she was rooting for me. And you don't really see that in other sports. It's really something special."

---

**Climbing:** I remember seeing videos of the two of you training together. I'm guessing you've probably been friends for a while, but your friendship has become more public lately. It certainly made for a special Olympic moment.

**Garnbret:** "Yeah, it was really special. Like it was in Tokyo, that picture with Miho [Nonaka] and Akiyo [Noguchi], this situation with Brooke felt similar. It was really heartwarming."

---

**Climbing:** There was also a recent video of you climbing in Fontainebleau—and back in 2022 you were famously projecting *La Dura Dura*. Are there more outdoor projects in your future?

**Garnbret:** "Yeah, I do have a lot of projects in my mind—some bouldering, some lead—and, of course, I want to do them all, but there's not enough time."

Her immediate priority was clear: "First, my next goal, I will be doing the World Cup in Koper. It's a home competition, so I'll be doing that. But after that, I will be more or less rock climbing."

---

**Climbing:** Is there a possibility that you'll take some time off of the World Cup circuit like you did after the Tokyo Olympics?

**Garnbret:** "Yeah, I mean, me and Roman haven't really shaped the plan yet for next year. I think it's too soon to say; it's right after the Olympics, and I need some time to decompress, to relax, and forget about training and competitions."

Her focus has shifted: "Right now I want to focus more on rock climbing, to be in nature. There's no time pressure [outside], no pressure at all—you just enjoy and just climb with friends."

### 原文全文

**Systematic Preparation Strategy**

Garnbret and her coach Roman Krajnik implemented an extraordinarily detailed preparatory program for the Tokyo Olympics. She explained her meticulous approach: "I had everything organized to the minute—everything. Nothing was left to chance."

The duo considered virtually every possible scenario that could arise during competition, from dealing with unexpectedly easy or difficult routes to recovering from poor performances in individual events.

**Physical Training Intensity**

Beginning when climbing was announced for the Olympics in 2016, Garnbret committed to an aggressive training regimen. She trained three times daily across six days per week, demonstrating her natural inclination to be constantly active. Initially skeptical about speed climbing, she ultimately embraced the discipline: "If you wanted to win a gold medal, you had to start training speed one way or another."

Her dedication paid off as she eventually reduced her speed time to under eight seconds—a personal best.

**Mental Resilience Under Pressure**

Garnbret's mental fortitude proved crucial when she performed poorly during Olympic speed qualifications. Rather than becoming discouraged, she relied on her competition experience to refocus. She advanced through qualifying rounds by dominating the bouldering section, which aligned with her core strengths.

**Embracing Failure as Training**

A key philosophy Garnbret emphasizes involves normalizing failure. She shared: "People think I never fail, but 99 percent of the training, I fail." She actively documents and shares her unsuccessful attempts on social media to demonstrate that even elite athletes constantly struggle and must learn from setbacks.

### 原文全文

Roman Krajnik has coached Slovenia's Janja Garnbret to two Olympic gold medals. Garnbret credits Krajnik as "the reason winning is even remotely possible."

**Krajnik's Coaching Journey**

Krajnik discovered climbing by chance at age 18 through a bouldering wall at his school gym. Frustrated by the lack of coaching available to him, he decided to become a coach himself around 1993–1994. He began with small groups and gradually expanded, coaching notable athletes including the Vidmar sisters before Janja approached him in 2019.

**Identifying Areas for Improvement**

Despite Garnbret's already elite status, Krajnik identified significant development opportunities:
- **Injury prevention:** He implemented physical therapy protocols to address recurring shoulder and knee problems
- **Technical weaknesses:** Garnbret struggled with slopers and slab climbing, partly due to overly stiff shoes
- **Continuous refinement:** Krajnik maintains that improvement is "a never-ending story"

**Philosophy on Talent vs. Training**

Krajnik believes talent comprises only "20 or 30 percent" of elite climbing success, with the remainder dependent on training, dedication, and mental fortitude. He emphasizes that talent alone cannot sustain competitive excellence in modern climbing.

**Measuring Success**

Rather than prioritizing medals, Krajnik defines success through athlete satisfaction and measurable monthly and yearly improvements. He describes competitive accolades as "just little rewards" for genuine progress.

**Coaching Style**

Krajnik adapts his methodology to individual athletes, rejecting one-size-fits-all training plans. He emphasizes environmental attunement, reciprocal commitment, and ongoing communication. He critiques coaches who apply identical philosophies across different athletes.

**What Janja Has Taught Him**

Garnbret's perfectionism stands out as exceptional. According to Krajnik, she approaches every training session with complete intensity, "like the first session," regardless of previous accomplishments.

**The Perfectionism Balance**

While perfectionism drives excellence, Krajnik acknowledges its potential downsides: "perfectionism could kill you if you are exaggerating it."

**Reflections on Modern Competition Climbing**

Krajnik expresses mixed feelings about climbing's evolution into an Olympic sport. He cherishes the "romantic" heritage of climbing but recognizes the sport has become professionalized. He worries that gym-based training has disconnected younger climbers from outdoor rock climbing's authentic experience. He particularly concerns himself with bouldering's trajectory toward gymnastic and acrobatic movements.

### 摘要 / 备注

> 教练 Roman Krajnik 深度人物报道。核心洞见：1）才能仅占 20-30%，其余靠训练和心理；2）Janja 的弱点：slopers 和 slab（2019年开始合作时）；3）教练理念——个性化、永无止境的进步；4）对攀岩被体操化的担忧。

**"Every Day is a New Battle"**

When asked about her climbing style, Garnbret describes herself as energized and present when performing at her best. "I'm a perfectionist, so I want to be great. It's always a new day."

**Managing Pressure and Doubt**

Despite her achievements, Garnbret experiences pressure from media, the climbing community, and fans worldwide. She manages this by focusing on the present moment. She admits to having doubts: "Am I ready enough? Did I train hard enough?" Yet she recognizes that ultimately, showing up and giving maximum effort is what matters.

**Training Regimen**

During competitive seasons (November to April), Garnbret trains six times weekly for four to five hours, with approximately 95% of that time spent on the wall itself. She supplements climbing practice with injury prevention work with her physiotherapist.

**Unique Qualities**

Garnbret attributes her success to her work ethic and perfectionism. She describes herself as someone who "still work like I haven't achieved anything."

**Inspiration and Legacy**

She values her role as a role model, wanting to demonstrate that "anything is possible." Her passion for climbing stems from the sport's constant variety and the physical-mental balance it requires.

**Future Plans**

"I will end my career when I stop having fun," she states, indicating her motivation remains intrinsic rather than external. She plans to eventually become a coach to mentor the next generation.

**Performance Details**

On future plans: "I've started projects on rock that I want to finish in the coming months."

**The Bigger Picture**

Garnbret's mastery of both Boulder and Lead disciplines challenges conventional training approaches where most elite climbers specialize. A Red Bull Studios feature film is expected in summer 2028.
