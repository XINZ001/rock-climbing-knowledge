**Early Life and Introduction to Climbing**

John Bachar (March 23, 1957 – July 5, 2009) was an American rock climber who became the defining figure of free solo climbing in the Yosemite Valley during the 1970s and 1980s. He grew up in Los Angeles, California, and began climbing at Stoney Point, a bouldering hot spot in the northern San Fernando Valley. After attending Westchester High School (graduating 1974), he enrolled at UCLA, where his father was a mathematics professor — but dropped out to pursue climbing full-time.

**Introduction to Free Soloing**

Bachar began soloing at Joshua Tree at age 16, initially on easy routes as a practical way to access climbing formations without rope setup. The turning point came through his mentor John Long, who issued a simple logical challenge on Double Cross (5.8): "Man, how many times would you fall if you toproped it 100 times?" When Bachar answered "zero," Long replied: "Alright then, we're on it!"

This conversation crystallized his commitment to the discipline. In 1975, Long introduced 18-year-old Bachar to free soloing in Yosemite, and they followed up with circuits of unroped climbing that linked together thousands of feet of rock.

**Major Free Solo Achievements**

Bachar's progression through increasingly difficult routes established the upper boundary of what was considered possible in free soloing:
- **New Dimensions (5.11a)** — considered outlandish at the time for an unroped ascent
- **Nabisco Wall via Butterballs (5.11c)** — 100-meter route, pushed his personal limits
- **Moratorium** — onsight free solo; he described this as crossing his comfort zone: "I felt like I got away with something rather than dominating and flowing with the rock like I usually do"
- **Baby Apes (5.12c)**
- **Father Figure (5.13a)** — his hardest free solo

**Bachar-Yerian Route**

European experiences in 1980 at Sport Scheck's climbing convention transformed his perspective. Climbing steep limestone in Germany (Chasin' the Trane, 5.12d) and Verdon (Pichenibule, 5.12c) influenced his vision for the now-famous **Bachar-Yerian** route at Tuolumne Meadows, one of the most dangerous trad routes in America.

**Death**

Bachar died on July 5, 2009, during a free solo attempt near his home in Mammoth Lakes, California. He was 52. Despite his extraordinary safety record over decades, he ultimately died doing what he had built his entire life around.

**Free Solo as Natural Expression**

Bachar described free soloing as feeling "so free, so easy, like it was the most natural form of climbing." He viewed solo climbing not as a stunt but as climbing in its purest state — direct engagement with the rock without the mediation of protection systems.

**Control as Core Philosophy**

Control was the essence of Bachar's approach. When control was not possible, he knew to back off. His philosophy: elevate your climbing skills to the level of the rock, instead of lowering the rock to your level. He believed in being strong enough not just to climb any route solo, but to be able to **down-climb out of trouble** — a safety principle that defined his entire approach.

He had studied kinesiology to create a highly disciplined training routine of pullups with a weight belt. He was famously obsessive about physical conditioning.

**Intrinsic Motivation**

Unlike climbers of later generations with sponsorships, Bachar climbed purely out of intrinsic love: "I knew I loved climbing when I was about 14." His driving force was curiosity: "I want to see how far I can go." He abandoned mathematics at UCLA to dedicate himself completely to climbing excellence.

**Traditional Climbing Ethics**

Bachar defined traditional climbing as "doing the first ascent from the ground up and onsight (no prior inspection or practicing from above)." He viewed ground-up first ascents as essential, providing climbers the ultimate challenge of encountering unknown terrain and improvising responses — skills that he believed sport climbing and rap-bolted routes systematically diminished.

He was strongly opposed to the spread of sport climbing ethics into traditional and adventure climbing areas, arguing it weakened climbers by removing the challenge of unknown terrain.

**Bachar Ladder**

As a training innovation, Bachar is credited with inventing the **Bachar ladder** — a rope ladder with rungs that can be angled and adjusted, allowing campus-board-style moves without a campus board. The device became widely adopted in climbing training and bears his name to this day.

**Footwear Philosophy**

As Acopa's head shoe designer, Bachar advocated against the trend of painfully tight climbing shoes: "You will climb better if your shoes are comfortable." He noted that in his era, climbing shoes were so painful they hindered concentration and enjoyment.

**Legacy**

Free solo legends including Alex Honnold, Lonnie Kauk, and Renan Ozturk have all commented on the inspiration they received from Bachar. His ethical stance on ground-up climbing and his insistence on total mastery before attempting a solo continue to influence how climbers think about preparation and risk.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

The **Bachar-Yerian** route at Tuolumne Meadows is widely regarded as one of the most committing and dangerous traditionally-protected routes in America. Named after John Bachar and Dave Yerian, the route features runout sections with extremely difficult moves and minimal protection — a direct physical expression of Bachar's philosophical commitment to ground-up, fully committed climbing.

The route's existence is itself a statement: that the standard of skill required to climb it safely is so high that only those who have truly earned the ability should attempt it. Bachar intended routes like this as a filter — not accessible to those who hadn't put in the years of work.

Contemporaries Ron Kauk and Peter Croft were regular partners and rivals in this Yosemite golden age, pushing standards collectively through friendly competition and mutual respect. The era they defined — roughly 1975–1990 — established Yosemite as the global center of free soloing culture.
