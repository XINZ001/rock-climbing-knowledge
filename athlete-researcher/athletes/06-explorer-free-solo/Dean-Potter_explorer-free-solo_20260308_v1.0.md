### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

**Early Life**

Dean Potter was born in 1972 at Fort Leavenworth, Kansas, to an Army officer father. He grew up in New Hampshire, taught himself to climb in 10th grade, and attended the University of New Hampshire, where he rowed varsity crew. Potter quit college to pursue climbing full-time.

He was six-foot-five with a lanky frame ideally suited for long reaches on rock faces. Nicknamed the "Dark Wizard" for his brooding intensity and unconventional approach, Potter became one of the most influential aerialists and soloists of his generation.

**Philosophical Foundation**

Potter credited his parents for instilling the core principles that defined his climbing approach. He learned yoga practices as a child through his mother, drawing breathwork techniques that he applied to high-stakes climbing. From his father's military background and distance running, he absorbed training discipline.

His driving motivation was confronting his deepest fear:

> "This concept of turning dying into flying is a metaphor for my basic life principle."

He treated climbing and flying as "arts" rather than mere sports, pursuing them with the same commitment an artist brings to their craft.

**Free Solo Achievements**

| Year | Achievement |
|------|-------------|
| 1998 | Four-hour speed solo of the Regular Northwest Face on Half Dome — 95% free soloed |
| 2001 | First Yosemite Triple Crown linkup with Timmy O'Neill: Half Dome, Mt. Watkins, El Capitan — one day |
| 2002 | First free solo of Fitz Roy via Supercanaleta, Patagonia |
| 2006 | Free solo of Delicate Arch, Arches National Park (controversial) |
| 2008 | FreeBASE ascent of the Eiger's Deep Blue Sea route (5.12+) — first person to FreeBASE the Eiger |
| 2010 | Speed record on The Nose of El Capitan with Sean Leary: 2 hours 36 minutes 45 seconds |

He also free soloed Separate Reality (5.11d) five consecutive times, Heaven (5.12d), A Dog's Roof (5.12b), and Astroman (IV 5.11c).

**The FreeBASE Invention**

Potter invented "FreeBASE" climbing — a revolutionary variation where climbers wear a BASE parachute rig while free soloing. In the event of a fall, the climber can deploy the parachute and survive. This concept allowed Potter to tackle extremely exposed pitches with a minimal backup option.

He described the concept as extending the frontier of what was possible: "Being allowed to travel in nature in a way that doesn't harm the environment shouldn't be illegal." FreeBASE was Potter's attempt to push the upper boundary of free soloing while maintaining a thread of survivability.

**Highlining**

Potter was introduced to slacklining by Charles Victor Tucker III ("Chongo"). He popularized highline walking across extreme heights, completing multiple crossings without a safety lanyard or backup line — a direct parallel to his free soloing philosophy applied to a different medium.

**Controversy: Delicate Arch (2006)**

Potter's free solo of Delicate Arch in Arches National Park sparked significant backlash. Though technically legal at the time, the climb's publicity led to expanded climbing restrictions in the park and cost Potter his Patagonia sponsorship. The incident exemplified the tension between a free soloist's drive to push limits and the broader climbing community's concerns about public relations and ethics.

**Personality and Legacy**

Climbing writer John Long observed: "Dean Potter was an event… A force of nature" — noting Potter's unprecedented achievement in mastering three adventure disciplines (climbing, highlining, wingsuit BASE jumping) simultaneously.

Colleague Cedar Wright characterized him as living "right on the edge," adding: "You don't meet many old wingsuit BASE jumpers."

Potter won the Laureus World Action Sportsperson of the Year award in 2003.

**Death**

On May 16–17, 2015, Potter and Graham Hunt (29) died attempting a proximity wingsuit flight from Taft Point above Yosemite Valley. Their descent line was new and uncertain. Before the jump, Potter had left behind his constant companion — his miniature Australian cattle dog Whisper — recognizing the heightened risks of the unfamiliar line.

Potter's greatest fear — falling to his death — ultimately came true in pursuit of the boundary-pushing lifestyle he had pioneered.

**On Risk and Purpose**

Potter consistently articulated a philosophy of using extreme risk as a tool for self-discovery:

> "This concept of turning dying into flying is a metaphor for my basic life principle."

He believed the confrontation with mortality sharpened his awareness and deepened his engagement with life. Rather than avoiding risk, he sought to transform his relationship with it.

**On Freedom and Nature**

> "Being allowed to travel in nature in a way that doesn't harm the environment shouldn't be illegal."

This statement — made in the context of BASE jumping regulations in national parks — reflects his broader worldview: that the human body moving through natural space is a form of art and expression deserving of protection.

**The Nature of Adventure**

Potter represented a new archetype in outdoor sports: the multi-discipline extreme adventurer who refuses to compartmentalize. He was simultaneously a world-class free soloist, a highline pioneer, and a wingsuit BASE jumper — a combination that had never existed before him and has not been replicated since.

Teammates and observers noted his unusual combination of physical intensity and philosophical depth. He was not merely performing feats — he was using them as a philosophical investigation.
