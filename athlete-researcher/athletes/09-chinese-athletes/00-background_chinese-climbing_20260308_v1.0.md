### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

**Origins: 1987**

China's organized sport climbing history begins in 1987, when the Chinese Mountaineering Association (中国登山协会) sent representatives to Japan to systematically study the sport. Upon return, China held its first climbing invitational in October of that year. 1987 is recognized as the founding year (元年) of sport climbing in China.

In 1993, the Chinese National Sports Commission officially classified climbing as a formal sport discipline, providing institutional recognition and enabling structured development.

**Early International Emergence (1990s)**

Through the 1990s, China's climbers developed within a small but growing domestic competition ecosystem. Speed climbing received attention because its standardized format — a fixed 15-meter wall, 5-degree overhang, standardized hold placement — allowed systematic training and measurable performance improvement. The first Chinese speed climbing competition was held in 1999, beginning a 25-year development arc.

Chen Xiaojie (陈小捷) became the first Chinese speed climber to stand on an international podium, establishing that the discipline was accessible for Chinese athletic development.

**The Zhong Qixin Era (2007–2015)**

The defining moment of China's early climbing history arrived at the 2007 World Championships: Zhong Qixin, age 18, won gold with a world record time of 8.76 seconds. Over the following eight years, Zhong won four World Championship titles, broke his own world record six times, and became the first and only speed climbing grand slam winner. China's reputation in the discipline was built almost entirely on this one extraordinary athlete.

**Structural Gap and the 2016–2019 Trough**

After Zhong Qixin's competitive peak, a talent gap emerged. From 2016 to 2019, China struggled to produce athletes capable of replicating his results. The national program recognized this as a systemic risk.

**2017: The Cross-Discipline Selection Initiative**

In August 2017, the National Sports Administration launched an unprecedented recruitment initiative targeting four newly-Olympic sports: climbing, surfing, skateboarding, and BMX. The program called for athletes from established sports disciplines — martial arts, gymnastics, track, swimming — to be evaluated for cross-discipline transfer. In total, 3,716 candidates applied; 440 were selected and placed in national training camps.

This initiative was transformative. Athletes including Long Jinbao (from sanda kickboxing) and Deng Lijuan (from track athletics) entered the climbing system through this pathway. Their pre-existing physical attributes — explosive power, coordinative capacity, trained mindset — accelerated the normally long development curve in speed climbing.

**Olympic Entry and National Growth (2019–2024)**

Sport climbing's Olympic debut at Tokyo 2020 (held in 2021) catalyzed national investment. The Chinese national sports system expanded training infrastructure, increased coaching staff, and invested in sports science support including AI-assisted video analysis. The number of climbing gyms in China grew to over 400, with an estimated 1 million participant visits annually and approximately 300,000 regular participants nationwide.

China's national team organized in partnership with 11 provincial sports associations, creating a federated talent pipeline for the national squad. Registered competitive climbers exceeded 300 athletes.

**Paris 2024: Historic Double Silver**

At the 2024 Paris Olympics, China earned two silver medals in speed climbing:
- Deng Lijuan: 6.18 seconds (Asian record), women's speed — China's first-ever Olympic climbing medal
- Wu Peng: 4.77 seconds, men's speed — first Chinese male Olympic climbing medal

This double silver represented the full cycle: from the 1987 founding, through the Zhong Qixin era, through the 2017 structural rebuild, to international medal performance at the Olympic level.

**2025 Continuation: Long Jianguo's World Championship**

In September 2025, Long Jianguo won the men's speed climbing World Championship in Seoul with a time of 4.80 seconds — the first Chinese male world champion in the discipline since Zhong Qixin's 2012 title, 13 years earlier. The result confirmed that China's speed climbing depth had been fully restored and potentially expanded beyond the single-champion model of the Zhong Qixin era.

**Structural Characteristics of Chinese Speed Climbing Success**

Several factors distinguish China's speed climbing development:

1. **Cross-discipline recruitment**: Systematically identifying athletes with elite physical baselines from other sports, then training them specifically for speed climbing mechanics.

2. **Standardized infrastructure**: Wide deployment of competition-specification speed walls across multiple training centers, enabling year-round simulation of competition conditions.

3. **Data-driven coaching**: Integration of video analysis and, from 2024, AI-assisted movement analysis to identify micro-inefficiencies in technique.

4. **Coaching continuity**: Zhong Qixin's transition from competitor to national head coach created direct transmission of elite competitive experience into the coaching program.

5. **Institutional commitment**: Sustained multi-year investment cycles rather than short-term performance expectations, allowing the 2017 cohort to develop fully before delivering results at Paris 2024.

**AI-Assisted Training Preparation**

Reports following China's Paris 2024 performance revealed that the national climbing team had incorporated AI-assisted video analysis into pre-Olympic preparation. The system analyzed athletes' movements on the speed wall — grip transitions, arm extension patterns, foot placement timing — and identified technical inefficiencies that are difficult to detect through standard coaching observation. This represented an extension of the "data-first" training culture that China's national sports system has increasingly adopted across disciplines.

**Speed Climbing as a National Science Project**

Chinese national sports officials described speed climbing preparation as an almost scientific engineering process: decomposing the 15-meter wall into segments, identifying the optimal movement pattern for each segment, and training athletes to replicate these sequences with sub-centimeter precision and millisecond consistency. The approach treats speed climbing less as an athletic contest of pure power and more as a precision performance problem.

**Coaching Team Structure for Paris**

The Paris coaching delegation for climbing was led by Zhong Qixin as speed group head coach. Under him, multiple technical coaches handled different aspects of preparation: video analysis, physical conditioning, technique refinement, and mental preparation. This multi-staff structure, previously uncommon in Chinese climbing, reflected the level of institutional investment the sport now commands.

**Athlete Welfare and Long-Term Development**

The national program explicitly emphasizes long-term athlete development over short-term results. Athletes in the 2017 cohort were given three to four full training cycles before being expected to deliver major international results — a structural patience that proved productive. Younger athletes like Luo Zhilu (born ~2005) are being developed on this same timeline, with their Paris 2024 appearance treated as educational exposure rather than peak performance expectation.
