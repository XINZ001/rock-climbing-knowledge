**Early Life and Entry into Climbing**

Zhong Qixin (钟齐鑫) grew up in China and came to speed climbing through China's national sports development system. His physical attributes — explosive power, fast neuromuscular response, and exceptional arm drive — made him an ideal candidate for the speed discipline.

**2007: World Championship Gold and World Record at Age 18**

In September 2007, at the IFSC Climbing World Championships in Avignon, France, an 18-year-old Zhong Qixin won the men's speed climbing gold medal with a time of 8.76 seconds — at the time a world record. The result was seismic: not only did China win a world title in a discipline previously dominated by Russia, but a teenager had simultaneously broken the world record in his first World Championship appearance.

This was the first time a Chinese athlete had ever held the speed climbing World Championship title. It signaled that China's speed climbing development, which had been building since the late 1990s, had reached international elite level.

**Four Consecutive World Championship Titles**

Zhong Qixin went on to win the IFSC Climbing World Championship in men's speed for four consecutive cycles: 2007, 2009, 2011, and 2012. Each time, he returned as the gold medalist. Between these victories, he broke his own world record six consecutive times — continuously lowering the global benchmark in an era of technological and training advancement.

**First and Only Speed Climbing Grand Slam**

Zhong Qixin is recognized as the first — and as of the time of his retirement from competition — only speed climber in history to complete the speed climbing grand slam: winning the World Championship, the World Cup annual title, and all major international speed invitational events in a single competitive cycle. This combination of titles represents the highest possible achievement in competitive speed climbing, and no other athlete has replicated the complete set.

In 2015, he won the IFSC World Cup annual overall title in speed, adding the season championship to his collection. His career spanned a period when world records dropped from above 10 seconds to below 6 seconds, and he was central to that evolution.

**The Paradox: Record-Holder Without Olympic Gold**

Zhong Qixin's career contained a notable irony: he held the world record multiple times and won four World Championship golds, yet never competed for an Olympic medal — because climbing was not in the Olympics during his competitive prime. The sport entered the Olympic program only at Tokyo 2020, by which time Zhong had retired from competition.

A 2023 North Beijing Daily profile captured his reflections: the discipline Zhong dominated for nearly a decade, which he pushed from 8.76 to sub-6-second times, was finally an Olympic sport — but the medal opportunity arrived for the generation after him.

**Transition to Coaching**

Following his competitive retirement, Zhong Qixin transitioned to coaching. He became the head coach of China's national speed climbing team, overseeing the development of the next generation: athletes including Wu Peng, Deng Lijuan, and Long Jinbao. At the 2024 Paris Olympics, Zhong Qixin traveled as the climbing team's coach — watching from the coaching position as Wu Peng and Deng Lijuan won China's first Olympic climbing medals in the discipline he had dominated for a decade.

His coaching role places him at the center of continuity in Chinese speed climbing: the man who set the standard as a competitor is now building the infrastructure, technique models, and athlete culture that produced Olympic medalists.

**Legacy**

Zhong Qixin's career defined an era in Chinese climbing and in global speed climbing. He transformed speed climbing from a Russian-dominated discipline into a genuinely international competition. His multiple world records demonstrated that the 10-second range was not a ceiling — it was merely a starting point. He created a template for Chinese speed climbing success that coaches and athletes have cited in the years since as the foundation for current achievements.

His career motto and philosophy centered on methodical progression: training rigorously, racing analytically, and setting measurable goals. The generation of Chinese speed climbers who followed — and who now win Olympic medals — trace their lineage directly to the national program Zhong helped legitimize.

**The 25-Year Arc (1999–2024)**

China's speed climbing program dates to 1999 — the year of the first speed climbing event in China. The arc from that origin to the 2024 Paris Olympics double silver represents exactly 25 years of systematic development.

The first phase of international success arrived with Chen Xiaojie (陈小捷), who became China's first international speed climbing medalist. This established that Chinese athletes could compete at world level, setting the foundation for Zhong Qixin's later dominance.

**The Zhong Qixin Era: World Record to 2015**

Zhong Qixin's 2007 breakthrough initiated the peak era of Chinese speed climbing. From 2007 through 2015, he drove the discipline's records to new lows and China's international standing to new highs. But behind him, the talent pipeline remained thin — Zhong Qixin was the exception, not the product of a systemic depth.

**Trough Period (2016–2019)**

After Zhong Qixin's competitive peak, China's speed climbing entered a difficult period from roughly 2016 to 2019. Without a successor at world level, results declined. The national sports authority recognized this structural weakness.

**The 2017 Cross-Discipline Selection Initiative**

In August 2017, the national sports authority launched a cross-discipline recruitment program targeting four new Olympic sports: climbing, surfing, skateboarding, and BMX. A total of 3,716 candidates from other sports backgrounds applied; 440 were selected for national training camps. This initiative — directly comparable to how Long Jinbao was recruited from sanda and Deng Lijuan from track — rebuilt China's climbing talent pipeline with athletes who had elite physical foundations but were open to new disciplines.

**The Return: Paris 2024**

The 2017 initiative produced results by 2023–2024. Wu Peng (4.77s silver), Deng Lijuan (6.18s silver, Asian record), and Long Jinbao (2023 World Championship silver) collectively validated the model. The Paris double silver was not a single athlete's breakthrough — it was the product of a systemic rebuild completed over seven years.
