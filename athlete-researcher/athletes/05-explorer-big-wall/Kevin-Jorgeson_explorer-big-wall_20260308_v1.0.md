### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

Kevin Jorgeson, born October 7, 1984, in Santa Rosa, California, is an American rock climber best known for his historic climbing achievements.

**Early Life**

Jorgeson's father, an employee of Santa Rosa Parks and Recreation, encouraged his passion for the outdoors. At age 11, when an indoor climbing facility opened in Santa Rosa, his father inspired him to begin climbing. He competed in international climbing contests starting at age 16 and graduated from Maria Carrillo High School in 2003. He later attended UC Berkeley to study civil engineering while continuing his climbing pursuits.

**The Dawn Wall Achievement**

Jorgeson's most significant accomplishment came in 2015 when he and climbing partner Tommy Caldwell "successfully completed the first-ever free climb of The Dawn Wall on the southeast face of El Capitan in Yosemite National Park." The pair completed this 3,000-foot climb between December 28, 2014, and January 14, 2015. The route featured "multiple pitches rated 5.14 in the Yosemite Decimal System," making it exceptionally challenging.

Jorgeson initially contacted Caldwell after watching the 2009 documentary *Progression*, and the two planned their ascent over several years.

**Later Work**

Beyond climbing, Jorgeson co-founded 1Climb, an organization introducing children to climbing, and worked toward opening Session, a climbing facility in Sonoma County. He also performs as a keynote speaker for major corporations.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

**The Route and the Challenge**

The Dawn Wall occupies the southeast face of El Capitan in Yosemite National Park—described as "probably the hardest big wall in the world." The free climb features 32 pitches across nearly 1,000 meters, with two pitches graded 5.14d (9a) and the majority exceeding 5.13. Tommy Caldwell first envisioned the free line in 2007; Jorgeson joined the project in 2009 after contacting Caldwell via a mutual friend following the documentary *Progression*.

**The 19-Day Ascent (December 27, 2014 – January 14, 2015)**

The pair started their final push on December 27, 2014. The climb took 19 days—far beyond a typical big-wall expedition—because both climbers had to free climb every pitch in sequence without falls.

**Pitch 15: The Pivotal Battle**

Jorgeson's most defining moment came on Pitch 15, a 60-foot section demanding precise footwork on microscopic crystal holds. While Caldwell climbed ahead and waited, Jorgeson fought Pitch 15 for seven days—repeatedly falling, shredding his fingertips on the razor-edged microholds, and taping up his fingers to protect the skin. During this period, Jorgeson's situation became a global media story.

His reported breakthrough statement: "I felt overwhelmed with relief." After finally sending Pitch 15 on January 9, Jorgeson immediately continued up-route to reunite with Caldwell, who had been waiting for his partner.

Caldwell waited rather than summiting alone—a key narrative element of the climb and documentary. The two summited together on January 14, 2015.

**Media and Global Recognition**

The climb attracted unprecedented mainstream media attention for a climbing achievement. President Barack Obama tweeted: "You remind us that anything is possible." The team subsequently appeared on the Ellen DeGeneres show, in Vanity Fair, and on major international news programs.

**The Documentary**

Josh Lowell and Peter Mortimer's 2017 documentary *The Dawn Wall* provides the most complete record of the ascent from both Caldwell's and Jorgeson's perspectives, including the skin management struggle on Pitch 15.

**Partnership Dynamics**

Caldwell described their dynamic as complementary: his intense focus balanced by Jorgeson's more relaxed approach. Their relationship evolved from a professional partnership into something defined by shared sacrifice—Caldwell's decision to wait for Jorgeson at the summit became iconic.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

During the Dawn Wall ascent, Jorgeson's battle with Pitch 15 became a case study in skin management at elite climbing level. The pitch required repeated contact with razor-edged crystal microholds that wore through finger skin within a few attempts.

**Jorgeson's Skin Protocol:**
- Taping individual finger segments between attempts to protect partial healing
- Complete rest days specifically for skin recovery
- Allowing 24-48 hours of recovery between attempts on the hardest pitches
- Monitoring skin thickness daily to judge readiness for attempts

The skin management challenge was so severe and public that it generated widespread mainstream media coverage, including explanations of climbing skin care in non-specialist outlets.
