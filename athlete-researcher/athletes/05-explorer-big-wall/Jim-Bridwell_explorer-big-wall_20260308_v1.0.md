### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

On June 21, 1975, Jim Bridwell, John Long, and Bill Westbay accomplished a groundbreaking feat by climbing El Capitan's Nose in a single day—completing the 1,000-meter route in 17 hours and 45 minutes.

**Historical Context**

Warren Harding had required 47 days to first ascend the Nose in 1958. By 1975, the route had become a classic big wall objective, yet even elite climbers typically needed three days minimum. The Nose-In-A-Day represented a revolutionary achievement for its era.

Bridwell, nicknamed "The Bird," was an exceptional all-around climber. The concept originated with Frank Sacherer, a visionary climber known for accomplishing seemingly impossible feats. According to Bridwell, Sacherer's idea had "been firmly lodged in the Bird's mind" for a decade.

**The Ascent**

The trio departed near midnight under a full moon. They moved quickly through memorized sequences, reaching Dolt Tower by 6:15 AM. By pitch 13, all three climbers experienced significant arm fatigue. John Long pushed through pumped arms, nearly falling 80 feet before successfully placing protection.

The most difficult section involved the final pitches and the famous Great Roof. Someone had recently removed most fixed pitons, forcing Bridwell to place new protection while climbing fast—an additional challenge under time pressure.

**Legacy**

The record stood for 11 years until Mark Bachar and Dale Croft reduced it to 10 hours 5 minutes. By 2018, Alex Honnold and Tommy Caldwell achieved a sub-two-hour ascent. Despite these advances, the average Nose ascent time remains approximately three days. Bridwell's 1975 ascent opened new perspectives for the period's leading climbers—before the advent of camming devices, this was a feat of pure fitness, memorization, and boldness.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

Jim Bridwell (July 29, 1944 – February 16, 2018) was an American rock climber and mountaineer, active from 1965 in Yosemite Valley, but later in Patagonia and Alaska.

Born in San Antonio, Texas, Bridwell was the son of Donald Bridwell, an airline pilot. He was an apprentice to Royal Robbins and Warren Harding, and later the unofficial leader of the Stonemasters—the defining climbing crew of Yosemite's 1970s golden era.

He was noted for pushing the standards of both aid climbing and big wall climbing, and later alpine climbing. Bridwell is credited with over 100 first ascents in Yosemite Valley. Beyond The Nose single-day ascent, he pioneered routes in Patagonia and Alaska.
