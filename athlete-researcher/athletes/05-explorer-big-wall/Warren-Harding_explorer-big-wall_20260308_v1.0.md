### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

When surveyors first observed Yosemite Valley, they described its granite peaks as "perfectly inaccessible." Warren Harding, a boisterous Californian, challenged this assessment by completing the first ascent of El Capitan on November 12, 1958—exactly 60 years before this article's publication.

**The Competitive Drive**

Prior to Harding's achievement, climbing El Capitan's nearly 3,000-foot face seemed unthinkable. The motivation came in 1957 when Harding's rival Royal Robbins successfully conquered Half Dome's northwest face. Harding's competitive instinct kicked in: "What we gonna do? Well, nothing but El Cap would do," he reflected years later.

**The Climb**

Working with rotating partners, Harding's team ascended the Nose route over 18 months using hundreds of handmade pitons and fixed ropes. This staging approach allowed climbers to resume their progress at established points.

The team encountered extraordinary equipment challenges—many cracks were too large for available gear, so Bill Feuerer built makeshift pitons from the leg of an old cast iron stove, creating the now-classic "Stovelegs" pitch. The National Park Service compelled them to stop until March due to crowds, and after partner Mark Powell suffered a compound leg fracture, Harding continued with whatever qualified climbers he could recruit.

On the summit day, freezing conditions greeted the exhausted team. Harding struggled fifteen hours through the night, hand-placing 28 expansion bolts up an overhanging headwall before topping out at 6 AM on November 12, 1958.

On the profound experience: "It was not at all clear to me who was the conqueror and who was the conquered."

Total investment: over 45 wall days spanned over 18 months. The final team: Warren Harding, Wayne Merry, Rich Calderwood, and George Whitmore.

**Legacy**

The ascent fundamentally transformed climbing possibilities and established El Capitan as the sport's premier objective. Contemporary climbers have dramatically accelerated the same route—Alex Honnold and Tommy Caldwell completed it in under two hours in 2018.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

Warren Harding (age 47) and Dean Caldwell (age 27) completed the inaugural ascent of the Dawn Wall—originally called "The Wall of Early Morning Light"—on El Capitan in Yosemite during late October and early November 1970.

The climbers had anticipated needing 12 days but ultimately spent 27 days on the wall. They established the route by drilling 330 bolts while managing severe hardships including running low on food supplies and enduring a four-day storm. They famously rejected a National Park Service rescue offer.

During their ascent, Harding documented their struggle in a note dropped in a tin can: "We must be the most miserable, wet, cold stinking wretches imaginable. But we're alive, really alive, like people seldom are."

The 330-bolt count and the use of aid climbing techniques made this ascent highly controversial. Royal Robbins, champion of clean climbing ethics, was so incensed that he began retroactively removing bolts on a second ascent—but reconsidered partway through, acknowledging the climb's raw commitment.

**Historical aftermath**: This climb was the controversial pinnacle of Harding's career, attracting both condemnation (from the clean climbing camp) and admiration (for sheer audacity). Forty-five years later, Tommy Caldwell and Kevin Jorgeson made the first free ascent of the same route in January 2015.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

Warren Harding (January 21, 1924 – February 27, 2002) was an American rock climber who became one of the most influential and controversial figures in Yosemite's Golden Age.

**Key First Ascents:**
- The Nose, El Capitan (November 12, 1958) — World first ascent; 45 wall days over 18 months; partners: Wayne Merry, Rich Calderwood, George Whitmore
- The Wall of Early Morning Light / Dawn Wall, El Capitan (1970) — 27 days; partner: Dean Caldwell; 330 bolts; refused rescue

**Style and Controversy:**
Harding's siege-style, bolt-heavy approach was the antithesis of Royal Robbins' clean climbing philosophy. Their rivalry defined the ethical debate of Yosemite climbing in the 1950s-70s. Harding's attitude was unapologetically hedonistic and performative—he presided over the "Lower Sierra Eating, Drinking and Farcing Society, founded on the principles of gluttony and sloth." He wrote a memoir titled "Downward Bound: A Mad! Guide to Rock Climbing" (1975).

**Legacy:**
Harding is credited with opening the era of big wall climbing. Without his 1958 Nose ascent, the entire subsequent history of El Capitan—from the free climbing revolution to Free Solo—would not exist. He was featured prominently in the "Valley Uprising" documentary (2014).
