### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

Royal Robbins was a pioneering rock climber who led the "Golden Age of Yosemite rock climbing." He revolutionized the sport by introducing "clean climbing"—using only removable nuts for protection instead of permanently drilled pitons. His first ascent of Nutcracker in 1967 marked the inaugural clean climb in the United States and sparked ongoing debate about climbing safety standards.

Born in 1935, Robbins faced a troubled youth. At age 12, he was "a fatherless train-hopper on his way to a life of crime." After juvenile detention, he redirected his energy toward the Boy Scouts at age 14. As he reflected, "The Scouts got me off the streets of Los Angeles" and introduced him to outdoor adventure.

During a Scouts backpacking trip to Yosemite, Robbins discovered rock climbing. He noted, "I had a little something else" as a climber compared to his average hiking abilities.

In 1969, Robbins achieved another landmark: he became the first person to solo climb El Capitan in Yosemite National Park.

Beyond his climbing accomplishments, Robbins fundamentally transformed climbing culture by advocating for preservation of natural rock formations. He "changed the culture of climbing to better protect the natural features," ensuring mountains remained pristine for future generations.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

Royal Robbins (1935–2017) was an influential American climber who pioneered big-wall rock climbing in Yosemite National Park during the 1950s–70s.

**Climbing Philosophy**: Robbins championed free climbing and clean ascents, advocating for minimal rock damage. His philosophy: the decision to place a single piton is a matter of "enormous importance" because "like a single word in a poem, it can affect the entire composition." He places emphasis on using equipment which is non-destructive to the mountain environment and favors what he calls "upward variations"—completing a climb using more stringent standards than used on the first ascent.

**Major First Ascents:**
- Open Book at Tahquitz (1952) — first 5.9 in the U.S.
- Regular Northwest Face on Half Dome (1957) — first Grade VI climb in America
- Salathé Wall on El Capitan (1961) — now considered "the world's greatest rock climb"
- North America Wall on El Capitan (1964)

**Dawn Wall Controversy**: Robbins opposed Warren Harding's bolt-heavy approach to the Dawn Wall (1970). During the 1971 Dawn Wall second ascent, Robbins began removing bolts but reconsidered partway through, acknowledging the climb's commitment and legitimacy despite his ethical disagreement.

**Later Life**: After developing psoriatic arthritis in 1978, Robbins became a prolific kayaker, completing first descents of California's "Triple Crown" rivers. He co-founded Royal Robbins clothing brand (1967) and authored influential climbing manuals including Basic Rockcraft (1971) and Advanced Rockcraft (1973). Robbins died in Modesto, California on March 14, 2017, at age 82.

**On ethics and legacy**: Robbins was at the forefront of a trend called "clean climbing," in which he used only removable nuts to hang his climbing rope for protection. He was an early advocate for "leaving no trace, minimizing the use of fixed ropes, and avoiding fanfare about achievements." National Geographic called him the "Father of Yosemite Rock Climbing."
