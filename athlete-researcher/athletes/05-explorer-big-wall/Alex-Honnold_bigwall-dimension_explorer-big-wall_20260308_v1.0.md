### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

**Visualization**

Honnold treats mental preparation as foundational to physical training. He visualizes climbs to experience emotions beforehand, stating: "I'm thinking about what it'll feel like...so that I don't experience them while I'm doing the climb." For his Taipei 101 climb (January 24, 2026), his preparation focused on simulating deep fatigue through targeted training.

**Self-Talk & Regulation**

Rather than motivational rhetoric, Honnold's internal dialogue emphasizes practical composure. When unexpected situations arise, he employs deep breathing and reality-based reframing: "Once you've registered that it's happened, it's already happened… you didn't fall off." This anchors him in the present moment and prevents panic escalation.

**Mindfulness & Acceptance**

Honnold reframes fear as a physical sensation indistinguishable from excitement. He observes these sensations without judgment, recognizing that "just because you're feeling something in your body doesn't necessarily have to have an impact on the way you act."

**Process-Focused Goals**

Success isn't merely reaching the summit—it's performing with dignity and enjoyment. He emphasizes crossing the finish line composed and smiling, prioritizing how he feels throughout rather than outcome alone.

**Present-Moment Awareness**

During climbs, Honnold concentrates exclusively on executing the next movement, deliberately ignoring external pressures and heights.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy**

Honnold emphasizes consistent daily effort over massive leaps: "Big goals are just the outgrowth of lots of little goals done consistently. If you do enough little things, something big happens."

**Journaling**

He has kept a training and climbing journal since 2005, logging nearly every climb—not to glorify results, but to track progress and maintain accountability through deliberate practice.

**Submaximal Training**

Honnold favors submaximal training allowing frequent sessions (3-4 weekly) rather than training to failure once or twice weekly, supporting better recovery and adaptation.

**Mental Framework for El Capitan Free Solo**

For the El Capitan free solo, Honnold employed a strategic approach:
- He memorized the hardest sections completely but relied on familiarity and pattern recognition for moderate terrain
- He spent months preparing in Yosemite, understanding seasonal conditions intimately
- His mental state during execution aimed for "autopilot"—executing practiced sequences without overthinking
- He pursued a flow state comparing it to "dancing or something, where you are just flowing over stone"

**Risk Philosophy**

"Easy free solos can actually be safer than difficult climbed-with-ropes scenarios, since rope climbing encourages pushing into unknown terrain. With free soloing, hesitation and conservative decision-making naturally protect against catastrophic mistakes."

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

On February 24, 2019, at the 91st Annual Academy Awards, *Free Solo* won the Oscar for Best Documentary Feature.

The film was directed by Elizabeth Chai Vasarhelyi and Jimmy Chin. *Free Solo* chronicles Alex Honnold's extraordinary solo, ropeless ascent of El Capitan in Yosemite—the iconic 3,000-foot granite wall. The film documents Honnold's free solo climb of the Freerider route (5.13a), a groundbreaking achievement on June 3, 2017.

**Production Scale**

The production team captured 700 hours of footage using 12 cameras, including cameramen on the ground, cameramen on the cliff face, remote trigger cameras, and a helicopter with a 1,000 mm lens to capture 4K video.

**Box Office**

In the five months after its release, the film grossed nearly $19 million at the box office and also won the British Academy of Film and Television Arts (BAFTA) award for best documentary.

**Achievement**

On June 3, 2017, Alex Honnold became the first person to ever ascend El Capitan without the use of ropes, a harness, or a safety net of any kind. The completion time was 3 hours and 56 minutes.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

*Alone on the Wall* chronicles Alex Honnold's extraordinary climbing achievements and his groundbreaking free solo ascent of El Capitan in Yosemite National Park on June 3, 2017. The book recounts how Honnold became "the first person to free solo Yosemite's El Capitan—to scale the wall without rope, a partner, or any protective gear."

**Content Structure**

The book features a dual narrative: half written by Honnold (in italics with old logbook notes) and half by David Roberts, offering commentary, background, and interviews with family and climbing partners. The expanded edition includes two new chapters documenting the El Capitan free solo.

**Reception**

Kirkus Reviews called it "an inspiringly intense memoir." UK Climbing noted that "Roberts' commentary holds the book together" while glimpses into Honnold's mind were "the most fascinating aspect." The New York Times described the El Cap free solo as "one of the great athletic feats of any kind, ever."

**National Geographic Quote**

National Geographic described Honnold's free solo as "the greatest feat of pure rock climbing in the history of the sport."
