**Climbing** is the activity of using one's hands, feet, or other parts of the body to ascend a steep topographical object that can range from the world's tallest mountains (e.g. the [eight thousanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eight_thousander "Eight thousander")) to small [boulders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering").[^1] Climbing is done for locomotion, sporting recreation, for competition, and is also done in trades that rely on ascension, such as construction and military operations. Climbing is done indoors and outdoors, on natural surfaces (e.g. [rock climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_climbing "Rock climbing") and [ice climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_climbing "Ice climbing")), and on artificial surfaces (e.g. [climbing walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_wall "Climbing wall") and [climbing gyms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_gym "Climbing gym")).

## Evolution as a sport

The sport of climbing evolved by [climbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_climbers_and_mountaineers "List of climbers and mountaineers") making [first ascents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_ascent "First ascent") of new types of [climbing routes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_route "Climbing route"), using new [climbing techniques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_technique "Climbing technique"), at ever-increasing [grades of difficulty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_\(climbing\) "Grade (climbing)"), with ever-improving pieces of [climbing equipment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_equipment "Climbing equipment"). [Guides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_guide "Mountain guide") and [guidebooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_guidebook "Climbing guidebook") were an important element in [developing the popularity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_rock_climbing "History of rock climbing") of the sport in the natural environment. Early pioneers included [Walter Bonatti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Bonatti "Walter Bonatti"), [Riccardo Cassin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riccardo_Cassin "Riccardo Cassin"), [Hermann Buhl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Buhl "Hermann Buhl"), and [Gaston Rébuffat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaston_R%C3%A9buffat "Gaston Rébuffat"), who were followed by and [Reinhold Messner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinhold_Messner "Reinhold Messner") and [Doug Scott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doug_Scott "Doug Scott"), and later by [Mick Fowler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mick_Fowler "Mick Fowler") and [Marko Prezelj](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marko_Prezelj "Marko Prezelj"), and [Ueli Steck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ueli_Steck "Ueli Steck").

Since the 1980s, the development of the safer format of [bolted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolt_\(climbing\) "Bolt (climbing)") [sport-climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing"), the wider availability of artificial climbing walls and climbing gyms, and the development of [competition climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_climbing "Competition climbing"), increased the popularity of rock climbing as a sport, and led to the emergence of professional rock climbers, such as [Wolfgang Güllich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_G%C3%BCllich "Wolfgang Güllich"), [Alexander Huber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Huber "Alexander Huber"), [Chris Sharma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Sharma "Chris Sharma"), [Adam Ondra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Ondra "Adam Ondra"), [Lynn Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lynn_Hill "Lynn Hill"), [Catherine Destivelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catherine_Destivelle "Catherine Destivelle"), and [Janja Garnbret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janja_Garnbret "Janja Garnbret").

Climbing became an [Olympic sport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olympic_sports "Olympic sports") for the first time in the 2021 Olympic Games in Tokyo (see [Sport climbing at the 2020 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing_at_the_2020_Summer_Olympics "Sport climbing at the 2020 Summer Olympics")) in that format that included [competition lead climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_lead_climbing "Competition lead climbing"), [competition bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_bouldering "Competition bouldering"), and [competition speed climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_speed_climbing "Competition speed climbing") disciplines; [competition ice climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_ice_climbing "Competition ice climbing") is not yet an Olympic sport.[^2]

## Rock-based

The sport of [rock climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_climbing "Rock climbing") can trace its [origins to the late 19th-century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_rock_climbing "History of rock climbing"), and has since developed into several major sub-disciplines. Single-pitch and multi-pitch (and big-wall) climbing, can be performed in varying styles (including aid, sport, traditional, free solo, and top-roping), while the standalone discipline of [bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering") (or boulder climbing) is, by definition, performed in a free-solo format.[^3] [^4]

- [Single pitch climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_\(climbing\) "Pitch (climbing)") means ascending climbs that are a single rope-length (up to 50-metres) while [multi-pitch climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-pitch_climbing "Multi-pitch climbing") (and [big wall climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_wall_climbing "Big wall climbing")) means ascending [routes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_route "Climbing route") that are many rope-lengths (even up to 1,000-metres). These two rock climbing sub-disciplines can be conducted in one of several ways:[^3] [^4]

- [Aid climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aid_climbing "Aid climbing") is a form of rock climbing that uses artificial aids such as [aiders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aider "Aider"), [pitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piton "Piton"), and other mechanical devices to assist in ascending a route. Much of rock climbing began as aid climbing, and even by the 1970s, many big wall routes required aid (e.g. *[The Nose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Nose_\(El_Capitan\) "The Nose (El Capitan)")* and the *[Salathé Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salath%C3%A9_Wall "Salathé Wall")*).[^5]

- [Sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") is a form of rock climbing that uses no artificial aids (which is known as [free climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing")), but does rely on permanent fixed [bolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolt_\(climbing\) "Bolt (climbing)") (or [pitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piton "Piton")), for use as [protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protection_\(climbing\) "Protection (climbing)") while climbing (but not as aid); was started in the 1980s in France and now makes up the world's [hardest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_grade_milestones_in_rock_climbing "List of grade milestones in rock climbing") climbs (e.g. *[Silence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silence_\(climb\) "Silence (climb)")*).[^6] [^7]

- [Traditional climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing") is a form of rock climbing that uses no artificial aids (and is thus free climbing) but unlike sport climbing, the climbers place removable protection such as [SCLDs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring-loaded_camming_device "Spring-loaded camming device") and [nuts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nut_\(climbing\) "Nut (climbing)") while ascending that are removed by the second climber; has many famous routes (e.g. *[Indian Face](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Face "Indian Face")*, *[Cobra Crack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobra_Crack "Cobra Crack")*).[^8]

- [Free soloing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_solo "Free solo") is a form of rock climbing that uses no artificial aids (and is thus also free climbing) and where the climber uses no protection (neither sport nor traditional); thus any fall while free soloing could be fatal; [deep-water soloing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep-water_soloing "Deep-water soloing") is a form of free soloing where a fall will result in landing into safe water. The 2017 free solo of *[Freerider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freerider_\(climb\) "Freerider (climb)")* became the Oscar-winning film, *[Free Solo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_Solo "Free Solo")*.[^9]

- [Top rope climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_rope_climbing "Top rope climbing") is a form of rock climbing that uses no artificial aids but as the sole form of protection, uses a pre-fixed rope secured to the top of the route (i.e. is used on single-pitches), and thus should the climber fall, they simply hang off the rope with no risk of any injury; it is not regarded as free climbing but is a popular and safe way to introduce people to free climbing (and common on [climbing walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_wall "Climbing wall")).[^10]

- [Bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering"): means ascending boulders or small outcrops with no artificial aids (free climbing) and due to the lower height, with no protection (making bouldering a form of free soloing); very tall boulders where a fall could be serious (i.e. up to 10-metres) are known as [highball bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering#Highball_bouldering "Bouldering"). Many [milestones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_grade_milestones_in_rock_climbing#Solved_by_men "List of grade milestones in rock climbing") in bouldering (e.g. *[Midnight Lightning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midnight_Lightning_\(climb\) "Midnight Lightning (climb)")*, *[Dreamtime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dreamtime_\(climb\) "Dreamtime (climb)")* and *[Burden of Dreams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burden_of_Dreams_\(climb\) "Burden of Dreams (climb)")*) were created by practitioners of bouldering and free climbing.

## Mountain-based

[Mountaineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountaineering "Mountaineering") as a form of recreation can trace its [origins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountaineering#History "Mountaineering") to an even earlier time than rock climbing, and by the mid 18th century mountaineering in alpine environmnets has become as established pastime. It has since developed into several major sub-disciplines, including:[^3]

- [Mountaineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountaineering "Mountaineering"): Ascending mountains, which can involve some rock or ice climbing, but unlike [alpine climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpine_climbing "Alpine climbing") can involve [support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expedition_style "Expedition style") and [fixed ropes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_rope "Fixed rope").

- [Alpine climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpine_climbing "Alpine climbing"): Ascending large routes that require rock, ice, and mixed climbing skills but with minimal equipment and no outside support.[^11]
- [Scrambling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scrambling "Scrambling"): Climbing rocky faces and ridges, which can include basic rock climbing, but is considered part of [hillwalking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hillwalking "Hillwalking").
- [Via ferrata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Via_ferrata "Via ferrata"): Ascending mountain routes using previously installed fixed steel cables, metal rungs, and ladders for protection and aid.

- [Ice climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_climbing "Ice climbing"): Ascending frozen water ice or hard alpine snow using equipment such as [ice axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_axe "Ice axe") and [crampons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crampon "Crampon"); usually in mountain settings.[^12]

- [Mixed climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_climbing "Mixed climbing"): Ascending routes using ice climbing equipment where there is both rock and ice (called [dry-tooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry-tooling "Dry-tooling") if there is no ice).[^12]

- [Solo climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solo_climbing "Solo climbing"): Ascending routes alone; can involve ropes ([roped solo climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roped_solo_climbing "Roped solo climbing")) and artificial aid; where no protection or aid is used, it is free soloing.

## Competition-based

[Competition climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_climbing "Competition climbing") (sometimes confusingly called "sport climbing"), is a regulated sport of 'competitive rock climbing' that originated in the 1980s, and which is done as [indoor climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indoor_climbing "Indoor climbing") on artificial [climbing walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_wall "Climbing wall"). The worldwide governing body for competition rock-climbing is [World Climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Climbing "World Climbing"), which is recognized by the [IOC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOC "IOC") and [GAISF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GAISF "GAISF"), and is a member of the International World Games Association ([IWGA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IWGA "IWGA")). Competition climbing has three parts:[^13] [^14] [^15]

- [Competition lead climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_lead_climbing "Competition lead climbing") is a form of competitive [lead climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead_climbing "Lead climbing") performed on an artificial bolted [sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") route.
- [Competition bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_bouldering "Competition bouldering") is a form of competitive [bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering") performed on a selection of artificial bouldering routes.
- [Competition speed climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_speed_climbing "Competition speed climbing") is a form of competitive [speed climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_climbing "Speed climbing") performed on a standardized artificial wall with a [top rope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_rope "Top rope").

[Competition ice climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_ice_climbing "Competition ice climbing") is a regulated sport of 'competitive ice climbing' that originated in the early 2000s, and which is done on outdoor or indoor climbing on artificial ice climbing walls that consist of ice and dry surfaces. The [UIAA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UIAA "UIAA") is the governing body for competition ice climbing worldwide and their events include a lead ice climbing discipline and a speed ice climbing discipline. Competition ice climbing is not as yet an Olympic Sport.[^16] [^17]

## Other recreational-based

- [Buildering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buildering "Buildering"): Ascending the exterior skeletons of buildings, typically without protective equipment (e.g. as free solo climbing by [Alain Robert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alain_Robert "Alain Robert")).
- [Canyoneering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canyoneering "Canyoneering"): Climbing along [canyons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canyons "Canyons") for sport or recreation.
- [Crane climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crane_climbing "Crane climbing"): An illicit act of climbing up [mechanical cranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crane_\(machine\) "Crane (machine)"), which is a form of buildering.
- [Grass climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grass_climbing "Grass climbing"): An older form of climbing when climbing steep but grassy mountainsides, often requiring ropes, was undertaken.
- [Mallakhamba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mallakhamba "Mallakhamba"): A traditional Indian sport that combines climbing a pole or rope with the performance of aerial [yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga_as_exercise "Yoga as exercise") and gymnastics.
- [Parkour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parkour "Parkour"): A sport based around smooth movement, including climbing, around urban landscapes.
- [Pole climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pole_climbing "Pole climbing"): Climbing poles and masts without equipment.
- [Rope climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rope_climbing "Rope climbing"): Climbing a short, thick rope for speed; not to be confused with *roped climbing*, as used in rock or ice climbing.
- [Stair climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stair_climbing "Stair climbing"): ascending elevation via stairs.
- [Tree climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_climbing "Tree climbing"): Recreationally ascending trees using ropes and other protective equipment.

## Commercial-based

- [Rope access](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rope_access "Rope access"): Industrial climbing, usually [abseiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abseiling "Abseiling"), as an alternative to [scaffolding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaffolding "Scaffolding") for short works on exposed structures.
- A [tower climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tower_climber "Tower climber") is a professional who climbs broadcasting or [telecommunication towers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunication_tower "Telecommunication tower") or masts for maintenance or repair.

## Climbing and health

[Therapeutic climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Therapeutic_climbing "Therapeutic climbing") refers to the structured use of climbing for mental and physical health benefits. It integrates psychotherapeutic principles such as goal setting, emotion regulation, and self-efficacy building into climbing sessions.[^18]

## International organizations and governing bodies

- [World Climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Federation_of_Sport_Climbing "International Federation of Sport Climbing") (WC) serves as the official governing body for [competition climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_climbing "Competition climbing") worldwide. It is recognized by the [International Olympic Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee "International Olympic Committee") (IOC) and the [Global Association of International Sports Federations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Association_of_International_Sports_Federations "Global Association of International Sports Federations") (GAISF) and oversees and regulates competitive climbing events in the disciplines of [lead climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead_climbing "Lead climbing"), [bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering"), and [speed climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_climbing "Speed climbing").[^15]
- The [International Mountaineering and Climbing Federation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Mountaineering_and_Climbing_Federation "International Mountaineering and Climbing Federation") (UIAA) is a long-standing body for mountaineering and [climbing equipment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_equipment "Climbing equipment"), and which also oversees [competition ice climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_ice_climbing "Competition ice climbing"), where it sets standards and guidelines for ice climbing events and promotes the sport's development.[^19]

## In film

Climbing has been the subject of both narrative and documentary films. Notable climbing films include *[Touching the Void](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Touching_the_Void_\(film\) "Touching the Void (film)")* (2003), *[Everest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Everest_\(2015_film\) "Everest (2015 film)")* (2015), *[Meru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meru_\(film\) "Meru (film)")* (2015), *[The Dawn Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Dawn_Wall "The Dawn Wall")* (2015), *[Free Solo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_Solo "Free Solo")* (2018), *[14 Peaks: Nothing Is Impossible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/14_Peaks:_Nothing_Is_Impossible "14 Peaks: Nothing Is Impossible")* (2021), and *[The Alpinist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Alpinist "The Alpinist")* (2021). The [Reel Rock Film Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reel_Rock_Film_Tour "Reel Rock Film Tour") is a traveling film festival that exclusively screens climbing and adventure films, and includes the *Reel Rock* climbing film series.[^20]
