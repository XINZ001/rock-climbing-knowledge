From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Board with wooden rungs

Training on a board

A **campus board** (or **pan Güllich**) is a training tool that has been widely adopted by [sport climbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") to improve their [plyometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plyometric "Plyometric") performance and led to dramatic improvements in [climbing technique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_technique "Climbing technique") in all rock climbing disciplines. The campus board was invented in 1988 by German climber [Wolfgang Güllich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_G%C3%BCllich "Wolfgang Güllich") to help him climb the world's hardest consensus-graded route at the time, _[Action Directe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action\_Directe\_(climb) "Action Directe (climb)")_, and has since become a standard training tool for climbers.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-Gripped2-1)

Other climbing training 'boards' have been developed since the campus board, including the **MoonBoard**, a small customized overhanging indoor climbing wall also for plyometric performance, and the **hangboard** (or also the **fingerboard**), a device for building up static strength, particularly in the fingers, but also in the arms.

Typically, a user ascends or descends the campus board using only their hands, and often leaping from hold to hold (i.e both hands are off the board while transitioning between holds). Campus boards can take a variety of different forms and may incorporate a variety of materials. The earliest campus boards, still used today, were made of horizontal thin slats or wood rails attached to an inclined board in a ladder-like configuration. Later versions have utilized bolt-on [climbing holds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_hold "Climbing hold") or sections of a pipe. A campus board is generally set at an overhanging angle of inclination. One consideration for selecting the angle of inclination is the avoidance of any interference that may result between the user's legs and the campus board or wall.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-Gripped2-1)[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-2)

There are a variety of training approaches that may be used with a campus board, but all of them are centered around the concept of [plyometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plyometric "Plyometric") training. As one example, a user may alternate the use of specific fingers to increase finger strength when ascending or descending the board. As another example, upper-body strength may be increased by utilizing large lunges between specific rails or holds of the campus board ("power throws"). Reactive training may be used to increase muscle recruitment rates by dynamically moving between the campus board rails simultaneously with one or both hands. Training on a campus board may result in better performance due to the improvement of motor training, increased finger strength on a variety of grips, and greater power and lock-off strength of the arms.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-Gripped2-1)

The campus board has been a topic of controversy especially for newer or younger climbers, as it risks causing injuries to the climber's tendons due to improper technique or fatigue. As such, the campus board is generally not recommended for more novice or younger climbers.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-Gripped2-1)

The first scientific analysis of campus board training was published in 2021.[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-3)

The campus board was invented by German climber [Wolfgang Güllich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_G%C3%BCllich "Wolfgang Güllich") in 1988 while he was training for a new extreme [sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") route called _[Action Directe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action\_Directe\_(climb) "Action Directe (climb)")_, which required extreme dynamic finger strength.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-Gripped2-1) The first campus board was hung at a university in a gym called "The Campus Centre" in [Nuremberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuremberg "Nuremberg").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-Gripped2-1) Hence the term "campus" has been applied to the name of the training board, training method, and style of climbing, or "campusing" in which only the user's hands and arms are used. In French and in Italian, the campus board is referred to as _pan Güllich_.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-4) Early adopters of the campus board included British climbers [Jerry Moffatt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt "Jerry Moffatt") and [Ben Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Moon_(rock_climber) "Ben Moon (rock climber)"), who trained with Gullich and his climbing partner [Kurt Albert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Albert "Kurt Albert"), at their German gym.[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-5)[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-6)

Other climbing boards
---------------------

Section of a MoonBoard

The MoonBoard is a rectangular 2.44-metre (8.0-foot) by 3.25-metre (10.7-foot) section of [climbing wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_wall "Climbing wall"), typically overhanging at 25 or 40 degrees, onto which [climbing holds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_hold "Climbing hold") have been fixed in a dense grid-like fashion, with modern MoonBoards having almost 200 individual holds.[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-7) The tool was first commercially manufactured by British climbers [Ben Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Moon_(climber) "Ben Moon (climber)") and Rich Simpson in 2005, as a copy of the tools that they and other leading British climbers were using at an informal indoor climbing gym called [The School Room](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_School_Room "The School Room") in [Sheffield, England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheffield,_England "Sheffield, England").[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-CL1-8) Moon created earlier versions from wood in the late 1980s and 1990s, which he built with British climber [Andy Pollitt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andy_Pollitt "Andy Pollitt") as rudimentary indoor extreme training climbing walls that could fit into the basement of their house in Sheffield.[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-CL1-8)

Moon further developed the product and in 2016 released a version with [LED lights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LED_light "LED light") on the climbing holds so that climbers could follow pre-set sequences that could be shared by MoonBoard users worldwide (i.e. the grid of holds are identical on each MoonBoard edition), and with almost 200 holds, the online database for MoonBoard users had over fifty thousand [graded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing) "Grade (climbing)") sequences for climbers to attempt.[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-CL1-8)

The [Kilter Board](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kilter_Board&action=edit&redlink=1 "Kilter Board (page does not exist)") system is a fixed-layout app-controlled climbing wall with illuminated holds. Like the MoonBoard, the Kilter Board was developed to create an International climbing system with a shared database where users can add problems and keep a log of their climbs. The Original Kilter Board layout was tested and refined for over a year before the first board was released in 2018. There are now over 170 thousand climbs in the Kilter Board database. The Kilter Board Fullride layout was released in 2020 and has an additional 18 thousand climbs. The Kilter Board system has won the Climbing Business Journal Favorite Board System award every year since its advent in 2021.[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-9)

After the development of the campus board, the hangboard (also called the fingerboard) was developed to focus on building static arm strength, and finger tendon strength. The hangboard is a popular training tool for all types of rock climbers and comes in many forms, shapes and materials.[[10]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board#cite_note-10)

Action Direct 24 years old
==========================

*   Thursday 17th September 2015

Sept 17 th; 2015

Twenty Four years ago, on Saturday 14 th September 1991, Wolfgang Gullich completed the first ascent of Action Direct F9a in the Frankenjura, Germany.

His ascent was the culmination of hard specific training sessions on what we now call a campus board. Although Action Direct is ‘only’ 12 metres long, it overhangs at roughly 45 degrees and involves jumping between single and double-digit pockets, side-pulls and pinches. Gullich suggested Action Direct was worthy of a grade X1, an unprecedented high grade at the time.

The year previous, Ben Moon had climbed Hubble F8c+ then but now graded F9a like Action Direct. Together, they were two of the world’s hardest climbs. Both climbs have acquired legendary status.

Sadly, Gullich died of his injuries sustained in a car crash on August 31 st, 1992.

Alex Megos celebrated Action Direct’s 24 years by doing his fifth ascent of the Frankenjura and world test-piece classic.

**Wolfgang Güllich** (24 October 1960 – 31 August 1992) was a German [rock climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_climbing "Rock climbing"), who is considered one of the greatest and most influential climbers in the [history of the sport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_rock_climbing#1980s "History of rock climbing").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Outside-4)[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-AD-5) Güllich dominated [sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") after his 1984 ascent of _Kanal im Rücken_, the [world's first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Redpointed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)")[redpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing) "Redpoint (climbing)") of an [8b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13d) route. He continued to set more "new hardest grade" breakthroughs than any other climber in sport climbing history, with _Punks in the Gym_ in 1985, the [world's first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Redpointed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)")[8b+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14a), _Wallstreet_ in 1987, the [world's first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Redpointed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)")[8c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14b), and with _[Action Directe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action\_Directe\_(climb) "Action Directe (climb)")_ in 1991, the [world's first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Redpointed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)")[9a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14d).[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Outside-4)

Güllich was the [first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Free-solo_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)") person to [free solo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_solo "Free solo") at grade [7c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.12d) with his 1986 ascent of _Weed Killer_, and in that same year did his iconic free solo of _[Separate Reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separate\_Reality\_(climbing\_route) "Separate Reality (climbing route)")_.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Outside-4) He made first ascents of important new [big wall climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_wall_climbing "Big wall climbing") routes on the [Trango Towers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trango_Towers "Trango Towers") and the [Paine Towers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cordillera_Paine "Cordillera Paine").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Outside-4) With long-time climbing partner [Kurt Albert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Albert "Kurt Albert"), he revolutionized the training techniques for sport climbers, and the introduction of the [campus board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board "Campus board") in particular.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Outside-4) Güllich carried the mantle of "world's strongest sport climber" until his death in a car accident at age 31.[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-NG-6)[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Outside-4)

Wolfgang Güllich, was born in 1960 in [Ludwigshafen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwigshafen "Ludwigshafen"), [West Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Germany "West Germany"); the first son of Ursula and Fritz (Snr) Güllich.[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Bio-7) His father introduced him to [aid climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aid_climbing "Aid climbing") at the age of 13,[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Bio-7) and by age 15, he was climbing almost every weekend in the [Südpfalz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palatinate_(region) "Palatinate (region)") region with his younger brother Fritz (who in 1978 would die in a climbing accident).[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Bio-7) After an encounter with [Reinhard Karl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinhard_Karl "Reinhard Karl"), a leading figure in German mountaineering at that time, Güllich decided to apply himself intensively to climbing, and to [free climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing") in particular – which at that time was [traditional climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing") as sport climbing was still not widely practiced.[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Bio-7)

Güllich quickly became one of the best climbers in his region, making the [first free ascent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_free_ascent "First free ascent") of the [aid route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aid_climbing "Aid climbing"), _Jubiläumsriss_ ([VII-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#UIAA "Grade (climbing)")), at the age of 16.[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-AD-5) In 1981, he left the sandstone Südpfalz region to live in the limestone Frankenjura region, where a group of leading German and British climbers were starting to raise standards, including German climber [Kurt Albert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Albert "Kurt Albert") and British climber [Jerry Moffatt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt "Jerry Moffatt"), who both become life-long friends and training partners of Güllich.[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Climbing10-8)[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9) In 1983, Güllich freed the first German grade IX+ [5.13a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(7c+) route, _Mister Magnesia_,[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-AD-5) weeks before Moffatt freed _Ekel_ (IX+), also in the Frankenjura.[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9)

He also completed most of the other hardest routes in the US at the time including _Equinox_[5.13a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(7c+) in [Joshua ### Approach and philosophy

Güllich avoided the [competition climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_climbing "Competition climbing") circuit throughout his career. He felt that "competitions are good for earning money, I see it as nothing more", and was not attracted to competing with other climbers, just with the challenge of a real route.[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9) Although Güllich enjoyed traveling to other locations and testing himself on their hardest routes, saying: "A 'successful' climber is someone who has done many hard routes in many areas, not just a local specialist",[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9) he was most motivated by creating new routes, saying: "if climbing is an art, then creativity is its main component".[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9)

Güllich maintained an intensive and scientific approach to training for climbing, sometimes taking on specific training techniques for individual climbs.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1) While training for _Action Directe_, Güllich invented the [campus boarding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board "Campus board") to develop [plyometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plyometric "Plyometric") strength in his fingers and arms.[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM1-2)[[16]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Gripped2-16) Güllich co-authored with Andreas Kubin a 192-page German book on training for sport climbing called _Sportklettern heute_ (1986), and was an early advocate of [bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering") as a way to improve his technique and build performance, saying "the hardest routes are extended boulder problems";[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9) he also studied the use of the "[deadpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_climbing_terms#deadpoint "Glossary of climbing terms")".[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9) Güllich's advice would often extend beyond the physical, and was a source of notable quotes including: "A man doesn't go to drink coffee after climbing, coffee is integral part of the climbing".[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9)

Personal life and death
-----------------------

Wolfgang Güllich memorial stone

After moving to the Frankenjura in 1981, Güllich spent eleven years sharing an apartment, which included a gym in the cellar, with his life-long climbing partner Kurt Albert.[[17]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Guardian-17)[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Climbing10-8) The pair became famous for their hospitality and generosity to other international sport climbers.[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Climbing10-8)_[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Guardian "The Guardian")_ said: "Their flat became a meeting house for climbers from all over the world, testament to the ability of both men to make friends wherever they went".[[17]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Guardian-17)_[Climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing\_(magazine) "Climbing (magazine)")_ said of their apartment, "[it] became one of the most significant addresses in free-climbing history. It was essentially an open-house for the best sport climbers of the 1980s, people like [Ben Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Moon_(rock_climber) "Ben Moon (rock climber)"), Ben Masterson, [Ron Fawcett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Fawcett "Ron Fawcett"), [Ron Kauk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk "Ron Kauk") and [John Bachar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bachar "John Bachar")", and adding: "in an era before the Internet, their place was a hub of ideas and information".[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Climbing10-8)

In 1990, he met nurse Annette Favery whom he married one year later; Annette was photographed belaying him on his famous first free ascent of [Action Directe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_Directe_(climb) "Action Directe (climb)") on 14 September 1991;[[13]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-RI-13) which happened just five days after their wedding.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)

He was a climbing double for [Sylvester Stallone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester_Stallone "Sylvester Stallone") in the 1993 movie _[Cliffhanger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cliffhanger\_(1993\_film) "Cliffhanger (1993 film)")_, along with [Ron Kauk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk "Ron Kauk").[[18]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Cliff-18)

On 29 August 1992 Güllich fell asleep at the wheel of his car and veered off the [autobahn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autobahn "Autobahn") between [Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich "Munich") and [Nuremberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuremberg "Nuremberg") as he made his way home from an interview. Two days later on 31 August 1992 he died in a hospital in [Ingolstadt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ingolstadt "Ingolstadt"), never having regained consciousness. He was buried in [Obertrubach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obertrubach "Obertrubach").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)

Güllich is considered one of the greatest and most influential climbers in the [history of the sport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_rock_climbing "History of rock climbing"), and a pioneer in both technique and training.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1)[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-Outside-4)[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-AD-5) In 2013, when [National Geographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Geographic "National Geographic") announced [Adam Ondra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Ondra "Adam Ondra") as an "Adventurer of the Year", they noted that the title of "world's strongest climber" was held by Güllich in the 1980s and early 1990s, then passed to [Chris Sharma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Sharma "Chris Sharma") in 2001, and to Ondra in 2013; National Geographic said of Güllich: "In the '80s and early '90s German climber Wolfgang Güllich almost singlehandedly bumped the highest grade in rock climbing from 5.13d to 5.14d".[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-NG-6) In 2018, when Beth Wald wrote a preface to her 1987 _[Climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing\_(magazine) "Climbing (magazine)")_ interview with Güllich for the book _Vantage Point: 50 Years of the Best Climbing Stories Ever Told_, she introduced him as: "One of the finest rock climbers of all time the German Wolfgang Güllich developed cutting-edge training regimes that today have become commonplace...".[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-BethWald-9) In 2022, on the 30th anniversary of his death, _PlanetMountain_ called him: "A truly visionary figure, his influence extends well beyond sport climbing and even today his routes in far-flung corners of the globe such as Patagonia and the Karakorum are considered absolute milestones in big wall climbing in the high mountains".[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-PM10-1) In 2022, _[Die Zeit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Die\_Zeit "Die Zeit")_ called Güllich, "One of the best unknown athletes that have ever existed in Germany", noting that rock climbing was still a developing sport in the country.[[19]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich#cite_note-19)
