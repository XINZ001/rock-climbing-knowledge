**John Gill** (born 16 Feb 1937) is an American [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") who has achieved recognition for his [rock-climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock-climbing "Rock-climbing"). He is widely considered to be the father of American [bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-sherman-1)[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-2)[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-3)

Early life and professional career
----------------------------------

As a child, Gill lived in several southern U.S. cities, including [Atlanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlanta "Atlanta"), Georgia, where he graduated from Bass High School in 1954. He attended [Georgia Tech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_Tech "Georgia Tech") from 1954 to 1956, and graduated from the [University of Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Georgia "University of Georgia") with a degree in mathematics in 1958. He entered the [U.S. Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USAF "USAF") as a second lieutenant, and also attended a special graduate meteorology program at the [University of Chicago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Chicago "University of Chicago") in 1958 and 1959. He was assigned to [Glasgow AFB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glasgow_AFB "Glasgow AFB") in Montana until 1962, and resigned from the [USAF Reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USAF_Reserves "USAF Reserves") as a [captain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captain_(land) "Captain (land)") several years later.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-Master-4)

After obtaining a master's degree in mathematics from the [University of Alabama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Alabama "University of Alabama") in 1964, Gill became an instructor at [Murray State University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murray_State_University "Murray State University") from 1964 to 1967. In 1967 he enrolled as a graduate student at [Colorado State University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colorado_State_University "Colorado State University"), and received his PhD in classical [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") in 1971. His dissertation on [Möbius transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation "Möbius transformation") was supervised by Arne Magnus.[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-5)

During his career as a college professor, Gill wrote approximately thirty research papers on the analytic theory of [continued fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continued_fraction "Continued fraction"), [complex functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functions_of_a_complex_variable "Functions of a complex variable"), [linear fractional transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_fractional_transformations "Linear fractional transformations"), and related topics.[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-6)[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-7)[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-8)[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-9) He also started a minor mathematical journal entitled _Communications in the Analytic Theory of Continued Fractions_ with John McCabe of [St Andrews University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St_Andrews_University "St Andrews University"),[[10]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-10) served terms as mathematics department chair, president of the Sigma Xi (research) Club, Board of Governors of the Mathematical Association of America, and received an Outstanding Faculty Award. Gill retired as professor of mathematics from the [University of Southern Colorado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Southern_Colorado "University of Southern Colorado") in 2000.[[11]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-11)

John Gill, performing a dynamic move at [Pennyrile Forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pennyrile_State_Forest "Pennyrile State Forest"), KY in the mid-1960s.

John Gill began mountain and rock climbing in 1953 as a [traditional climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing"). By the mid-1950s he had begun to specialize in very short, acrobatic routes on outcrops and boulders, establishing problems in the 1950s and early 1960s considerably harder than those existing at the time.

### A modern perspective

Being a gymnast and thinking of climbing as an extension of [gymnastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gymnastics "Gymnastics") rather than hiking, in the mid-1950s he introduced the use of gymnastic [chalk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalk "Chalk") into American rock climbing. The use of chalk then spread internationally throughout the climbing world. At the same time he introduced [controlled dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_climbing_terms#dynamic_motion "Glossary of climbing terms"), recommending it as a technique of choice, as well as one of necessity. As for bouldering specifically, Gill's article, "The Art of Bouldering", published in the journal of the American Alpine Club in 1969 [[12]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-12) describes the sport of bouldering, the modern version of an activity that in the past had been viewed as informal training for longer climbs.

According to _[Alpinist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpinist\_(magazine) "Alpinist (magazine)")_ magazine, "_His introduction of chalk and dynamic movement marked the beginning of modern climbing in America._"[[13]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-Alpinist-13)

Gill's climbing style showed the influence of formal gymnastics. He emphasized form and grace of motion over simple efficiency, the cornerstone of contemporary climbing. His performances, as recorded on film taken when he was forty (_[Disciples of Gill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disciples\_of\_Gill "Disciples of Gill")_, 2009), [2013 John Gill Exercising (a)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=whBH5YyY4P8), and forty-five [John Gill bouldering at age 45 from "On the Rocks" - movie](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8FdMLil9lNU), demonstrate straight lines and minimal contact with the rock. He preferred an application of strength and sometimes avoided efficient moves like the [heel hook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_climbing_terms "Glossary of climbing terms"), which he considered unaesthetic. Gill's approach to bouldering – artistic style being on par with difficulty – was rarely followed by climbers of his generation and is considered unusual today, difficulty remaining paramount. He also practiced bouldering as a form of [moving meditation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meditation "Meditation").[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-Master-4)

Although certainly not the first serious boulderer — his notable predecessors include [Oscar Eckenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscar_Eckenstein "Oscar Eckenstein") (1859–1921) and [Pierre Allain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Allain "Pierre Allain") (1904–2000) — Gill was probably the first climber in the history of rock climbing to make bouldering his primary specialty and to advocate acceptance of bouldering as a legitimate sport in its own right. His focus on bouldering and the high difficulty level climbs he established inspired a number of traditional climbers to take a more serious view of the sport, which, for the most part had been seen merely as training for longer roped climbs.

After retiring, Gill spent several years researching the origins of rock climbing, particularly bouldering. He also compiled a chronicle of bodyweight feats associated with climbing. These results, along with a history of gymnastic [rope climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rope_climbing "Rope climbing"), may be found on his website.[[14]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-auto-14)

Gill received the 2008 [American Alpine Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Alpine_Club "American Alpine Club")'s _Pinnacle Award_ for outstanding climbing achievement.[[15]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-15) He received one of the club's highest awards, _Honorary Membership_, in 2023.[[16]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-16)

### Early bouldering & short climbs

Gill on Red Cross Rock Eliminate V9. With initial fingertip hold on the right the problem is V7.

In the [Tetons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetons "Tetons"), in 1958, John Gill climbed a short route on _Baxter's Pinnacle_ that lies in the [5.10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing) "Grade (climbing)") realm, before that grade was formally recognized — one of the first to be done in America.[[17]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-Wizards-17) By the end of the 1950s, Gill had reached what would now be considered [V9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(bouldering) "Grade (bouldering)") levels on a few [eliminate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_climbing_terms#eliminate "Glossary of climbing terms") boulder problems. (He later stated he probably never progressed beyond [V10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(bouldering) "Grade (bouldering)") throughout his climbing career.[[14]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-auto-14)).

Two of his problems on Red Cross Rock in the [Tetons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetons "Tetons") — a [V8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(bouldering) "Grade (bouldering)") in 1957 and a [V9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(bouldering) "Grade (bouldering)") in 1959 — set new standards of bouldering difficulty. And his 1961 route on a steep face of a small granite spire named the _Thimble_[[18]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-18) ([Needles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Hills "Black Hills") of South Dakota) — an unrehearsed and unroped 30-foot [5.12a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing) "Grade (climbing)")[free-solo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-soloing "Free-soloing") climb (or [V4 or V5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(bouldering) "Grade (bouldering)")[highball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering#Bouldering_Terms "Bouldering"))— is considered one of the great classics of modern climbing, and — if considered a climb (as Gill did) — may well be the first at the [5.12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing) "Grade (climbing)") grade.[[17]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-Wizards-17)[[19]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-19) Gill climbed the route without the benefit of modern climbing shoes, significantly increasing the difficulty of the climb.

In the 1950s, John Gill introduced a very early – if not the first – grading system specifically designed for bouldering and not restricted to a particular area.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-sherman-1) The system, [(B1, B2, B3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(bouldering) "Grade (bouldering)"), had two subjective levels of difficulty, and one objective level, and was predicated on prevailing and future standards attained in [traditional rock climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing"). The introduction of [sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") some twenty years later and more intense competition weakened the philosophical underpinnings of the three-tiered structure, although climbers such as [Jim Holloway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Holloway_(climber) "Jim Holloway (climber)") adopted _personal_ three-level systems similar to Gill's. Today, Gill's B-system is rarely used, abandoned in favor of open-ended grading systems such as the [V-scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-scale "V-scale").

Longer climbs - free solo explorations
--------------------------------------

From his early ascent of the east face of Longs Peak in 1954, throughout his climbing career until he left the sport in the early 2000s, a recurring theme of Gill's climbing was exploratory free soloing (at times with a light rappel rope) - usually, but not always, at moderate levels of difficulty. In fact, the Thimble – which he considered a climb and not a boulder problem - was a test of how far he was willing to go in that direction.[[20]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-20)[[21]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-21) Once he completed a route (most were unreported) he might return to it, perhaps many times, to enjoy the continuous movement and flow of the climb.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-Master-4)

Gymnastics and strength exercises
---------------------------------

John Gill, performing a one arm [front lever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Front_lever "Front lever") in the late 1960s. Gill is known for his applications of gymnastics to rock climbing.

As an amateur gymnast in the 1950s, 6'2" and 180 pounds, Gill specialized in the competitive [rope climb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rope_climbing "Rope climbing") and the [still rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_(gymnastics) "Rings (gymnastics)"), achieving a time of 3.4 seconds for the 20' rope climb (from seated position on the floor, arms only). The existing record - set in the 1950s - is 2.8 seconds.[[22]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-22) He accomplished a number of difficult stunts on the rings, including inverted and olympic crosses, giant swings, and slow pulls from hang to handstand. He also engaged in what are now called [bodyweight exercises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bodyweight_exercise "Bodyweight exercise") similar to gymnastics, achieving seven one-arm pullups with the right arm and five with the left, several one-finger one-arm pullups, one-arm pullups carrying twenty pounds, one-arm pullups on a one-half inch ledge, and one-arm front levers.[[14]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-auto-14)[[23]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gill_(climber)#cite_note-23)

John Gill (1937) is a pivotal figure in the history of bouldering and is often regarded as the “father of modern bouldering.”

A short summary of the influence of John Gill father of modern bouldering on bouldering.

Here’s an overview of his life and achievements:

**Early Life and Climbing Beginnings:**
---------------------------------------

John Gill was born on December 21, 1937, in Abington, Pennsylvania. His climbing journey began in the late 1950s, when climbers focused mostly on big walls and mountaineering. Gill introduced a new perspective, highlighting the challenges of smaller, intricate climbs.

Contributions to Bouldering
---------------------------

Gill shifted the climbing community’s focus toward bouldering as its own discipline. He popularized the idea of “boulder problems,” which are short, intense sequences on boulders or small rock formations. His innovative techniques, like dynamic movements and controlled body positioning, changed how climbers approached bouldering. He also introduced [chalk](https://boulderinginfo.online/introduction-to-bouldering/chalk/), drawing inspiration from his gymnastics background.

Notable Achievements
--------------------

Gill completed many groundbreaking first ascents, often considered extremely difficult for their time. He became the first climber to ascend problems that are now rated V9 (Fontainebleau 7A+), a notable achievement even today.

Theoretical Contributions:
--------------------------

Gill’s intellectual approach led to theoretical concepts like the “Bouldering Triangle.” This concept emphasizes balance, strength, and technique in climbing. In 1969, he wrote “[The art of Bouldering](https://publications.americanalpineclub.org/articles/12196935500/The-Art-of-Bouldering),” one of the first pieces recognizing bouldering as a distinct sport.

**Legacy**

Gill’s influence on bouldering endures. He inspired generations of climbers, and his techniques remain foundational today. He advocated for the sport’s purity, often avoiding climbing aids or chalk, focusing on raw skill and technique.

**Later Life**

Gill stayed active in the climbing community throughout his life. He participated in events and shared insights on climbing. His impact on bouldering is immeasurable. His pioneering spirit, technical innovations, and commitment to climbing laid the foundation for modern bouldering culture. He remains an iconic figure celebrated for his contributions to the sport.

Life and profession
-------------------

As a child, Gill moved frequently across southern cities, including Atlanta, Georgia. He graduated from Bass High School in 1954. He studied at Georgia Tech from 1954 to 1956 and earned a mathematics degree from the University of Georgia in 1958. Gill served as a second lieutenant in the U.S. Air Force and attended a meteorology program at the University of Chicago from 1958 to 1959. His military assignment in Glasgow AFB, Montana, lasted until 1962, after which he resigned as a captain.

In 1964, Gill earned a master’s degree in mathematics from the University of Alabama. He then taught at Murray State University until 1967. In 1967, he began a graduate program at Colorado State University, earning his PhD in classical complex analysis in 1971. His research on Möbius transformations was guided by Arne Magnus. Gill wrote about thirty research papers on topics like continued fractions and complex functions. He also started a minor journal, “Communications in the Analytic Theory of Continued Fractions,” with John McCabe from St. Andrews University. During his career, Gill served as department chair, Sigma Xi (research) Club president, and Board of Governors member for the Mathematical Association of America. He received an Outstanding Faculty Award and retired as a mathematics professor at the University of Southern Colorado in 2000.

Climbing
--------

Gill began climbing in 1953 as a traditional climber. By the mid-1950s, he specialized in short, acrobatic routes on boulders. During this time, he established problems that surpassed existing difficulty levels.

### Introduction of chalk to bouldering

Gill viewed climbing as an extension of gymnastics rather than hiking. In the mid-1950s, he introduced gymnastic [chalk](https://boulderinginfo.online/introduction-to-bouldering/chalk/) to American rock climbing. This practice later spread internationally. He also promoted controlled dynamics as both a preferred and necessary technique. Alpinist magazine noted that his introduction of chalk and dynamic movement marked the beginning of modern climbing in America.

### Gymnastics Influence

Gill’s climbing style drew heavily from gymnastics, prioritizing form and graceful motion. His film performances at ages forty and forty-five showcased precise lines and minimal contact with the rock. Gill saw bouldering as a blend of artistic expression and difficulty, diverging from the norms of his contemporaries. He considered it a form of moving meditation.

### John Gill father of modern bouldering

While not the first boulderer, Gill was the first to specialize in the discipline. He advocated for bouldering’s recognition as a legitimate sport. His dedication inspired traditional climbers to take the discipline seriously, transforming it from a training ground for roped climbs.

After retiring, Gill researched rock climbing’s origins, especially bouldering. He documented bodyweight feats associated with climbing and shared his findings online. In 2008, the American Alpine Club honored him with the Pinnacle Award, followed by Honorary Membership in 2023.

### First Boulders

In 1958, Gill ascended “Baxter’s Pinnacle” in the Tetons, a climb now rated 5.10. This ascent is one of America’s earliest instances of such difficulty. By the end of the decade, he reached levels now considered V9 on some boulder problems. He suggested he never surpassed V10 during his climbing career.

Gill’s achievements included two notable problems on Red Cross Rock — a V8 in 1957 and a V9 in 1959 — setting new benchmarks for bouldering. His 1961 ascent of the “Thimble,” a small granite spire in the Needles of South Dakota, was remarkable. This unroped 30-foot climb, rated at 5.12a (or considered a V4 or V5 highball), is a classic of modern climbing. If regarded as a formal climb, it may be the first at the 5.12 grade. Gill completed this ascent without modern climbing shoes, significantly increasing its difficulty.
