**Yvon Chouinard** (born November 9, 1938)[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-1) is an American rock climber, environmentalist, and businessman. His company, [Patagonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patagonia_(clothing) "Patagonia (clothing)"), sells outdoor products, outerwear, and food. He was named one of the [100 most influential people in the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_100 "Time 100") by _[Time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time\_(magazine) "Time (magazine)")_ magazine in 2023.[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-2)

Chouinard's father was a [French Canadian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Canadian "French Canadian") handyman, mechanic, and plumber. In 1947, Yvon and his family moved from [Lewiston, Maine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewiston,_Maine "Lewiston, Maine"), to Southern California. They were [Catholic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church").[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-3)

His early climbing partners included [Royal Robbins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Robbins "Royal Robbins") and [Tom Frost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Frost "Tom Frost").[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-outside-4) A [Sierra Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierra_Club "Sierra Club") member, in his youth he founded the Southern California Falconry Club, and it was his investigations of [falcon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon "Falcon")[aeries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerie_(nest) "Aerie (nest)") that led him to rock climbing.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-outside-4) To save money and to make adaptations for the way he was climbing, he decided to make his own climbing tools by teaching himself blacksmithing, and eventually started a business.[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChouinard2005-5)[_[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing\_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_]

Yosemite rock climber to leading alpinist
-----------------------------------------

Chouinard was one of the leading climbers of the "Golden Age of Yosemite Climbing." He was one of the protagonists of the film made about this era: _[Valley Uprising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sender\_Films#Valley\_Uprising\_(2014) "Sender Films")_ (2014). He participated in the first ascent of the North America Wall in 1964 (with [Royal Robbins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Robbins "Royal Robbins"), [Tom Frost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Frost "Tom Frost"), and [Chuck Pratt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chuck_Pratt "Chuck Pratt")), using no fixed ropes. The next year, his and TM Herbert's ascent of the Muir Wall on El Capitan improved the style of previous first ascents.[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1976[httpsarchiveorgdetailsclimbinginnortha0000jonepage362_362%E2%80%93363]-6) Chouinard became the most articulate advocate of the importance of [style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_climbing#style "Rock climbing"), the basis of modern rock climbing.

In 1961, he visited Western Canada with [Fred Beckey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Beckey "Fred Beckey"), and made several important first ascents, including the North Face of [Mount Edith Cavell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Edith_Cavell "Mount Edith Cavell") ([Rockies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Rockies "Canadian Rockies")), the Beckey-Chouinard Route on South Howser Tower in the [Bugaboos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bugaboos "Bugaboos") ([Purcell Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purcell_Mountains "Purcell Mountains")), and the North Face of [Mount Sir Donald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Sir_Donald "Mount Sir Donald") ([Selkirk Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selkirk_Mountains "Selkirk Mountains")). These climbs opened his eyes to the idea of applying Yosemite big-wall climbing techniques to mountain climbing, and his advocacy was important to modern, [high-grade alpinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing) "Grade (climbing)"). Also in 1961, he visited [Shawangunk Ridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shawangunk_Ridge "Shawangunk Ridge") for the first time, [freeclimbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing") the first pitch of Matinee (the hardest free climb done at Shawangunk Ridge at the time); and introducing [chrome-molybdenum steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/41xx_steel "41xx steel") pitons to the area, which revolutionized climbing protection. In 1968, he climbed [Cerro Fitzroy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Fitzroy "Mount Fitzroy") in [Patagonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patagonia "Patagonia") by a new route (The Californian Route, 3rd overall ascent of the mountain) with Dick Dorworth, [Chris Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Jones_(mountain_climber) "Chris Jones (mountain climber)"), Lito Tejada-Flores, and [Douglas Tompkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Tompkins "Douglas Tompkins").[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1976-7)[_[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing\_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_]

Chouinard has also traveled and climbed in the European Alps and in Pakistan.

Chouinard Equipment, Ltd.
-------------------------

In 1957, he bought a second-hand coal-fired forge, and started making hardened steel [pitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piton "Piton") for use in [Yosemite Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yosemite_Valley "Yosemite Valley"). Between time spent surfing and climbing, he sold pitons out of the back of his car to support himself. The improved pitons were a big factor in the birth of big-wall climbing from 1957 to 1960 in Yosemite. The success of his pitons caused him to found Chouinard Equipment, Ltd.[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-8)

In the late 1960s, Chouinard and business partner [Tom Frost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Frost "Tom Frost") began studying ice climbing equipment, and re-invented the basic tools ([crampons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crampons "Crampons") and ice axes) to perform on steeper ice. These new tools and his book _Climbing Ice_ (1978) started the modern sport of [ice climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_climbing "Ice climbing").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Around 1970, he became aware that the use of steel pitons made by his company was causing significant damage to the cracks of Yosemite. These pitons composed 70 percent of his income.[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-autogenerated2-9) In 1971 and 1972, Chouinard and Frost introduced new aluminum chockstones, called [Hexentrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexes_(climbing) "Hexes (climbing)") and Stoppers, along with the less successful steel Crack-n-Ups, and committed the company to the advocacy of the new tools and a new style of climbing called "[clean climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clean_climbing "Clean climbing")." This concept revolutionized rock climbing and led to further success of the company, despite cannibalizing the sales of pitons, formerly his most important product.

They applied for a U.S. patent on Hexentrics in 1974, and it was granted on April 6, 1976.[[10]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-10) These are still manufactured by [Black Diamond Equipment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Diamond_Equipment "Black Diamond Equipment").

In the latter 1960s, Chouinard attempted a number of significant technological and technique changes to ice climbing after trips to the [Alps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alps "Alps") in Europe and [Sierra Nevada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierra_Nevada "Sierra Nevada") ice gullies in autumn. He removed the flex from crampons, making them more rigid for front-pointing. He drew the taper of a rock hammer into a point for better ice purchase. He increased the cross section of ice screws while also using lighter materials. He experimented with pick and blade issues with ice axes. Prior to this, much of ice climbing was seen as mere step cutting. He attempted to replace hand ice picks (climbing type) with a small ice axe head called a Climaxe.

In 1989, Chouinard Equipment, Ltd. filed for bankruptcy protection in order to protect it from liability lawsuits. The hard assets of Chouinard Equipment, Ltd. were acquired by its employees through the [Chapter 11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapter_11 "Chapter 11") process, and the company was reestablished as [Black Diamond Equipment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Diamond_Equipment "Black Diamond Equipment"), Ltd.[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-autogenerated2-9)

Patagonia and environmentalism
------------------------------

Chouinard is most known for founding the clothing and gear company [Patagonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patagonia_(clothing) "Patagonia (clothing)"). Chouinard started to sell clothes by chance as a way to support his moderately profitable equipment business.[[11]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-11) In 1970 on a trip to Scotland, he purchased some rugby shirts and sold them with great success.[[12]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-Patagonia_company_history-12) From this small start, the Patagonia company developed a wide selection of rugged technical clothing. According to Chouinard, the intent of Patagonia is to make clothes for people under the rugged, southern Andes/Cape Horn conditions of places like Patagonia.[[13]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-13)

Recognizing that the financial success of the company provided the opportunity to also achieve personal goals, Chouinard committed the company to being an outstanding place to work, and to be an important resource for environmental activism. In 1984, Patagonia opened an on-site cafeteria offering "healthy, mostly vegetarian food," and started providing on-site child care.[[14]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-14) In 1986, Chouinard committed the company to "[tithing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tithe "Tithe")" for environmental activism, committing one percent of sales or ten percent of profits, whichever is the greater. The commitment included paying employees working on local environmental projects so they could commit their efforts full-time.

In the early 1990s, an environmental audit of Patagonia revealed the surprising result (at the time), that corporate [cotton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton "Cotton"), although it was a natural material, had a heavy environmental footprint. In 1996, Chouinard committed the company to using all organic cotton.[[15]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-15)

In 2002, Yvon Chouinard founded [1% for the Planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1%25_For_the_Planet "1% For the Planet") and Patagonia became the first business to commit 1% of annual sales to the environment.[[16]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-16)

In 2014, Patagonia supported the advocacy documentary film _[DamNation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DamNation "DamNation")_, which is about changing attitudes in America towards its dams. Chouinard was the executive producer of the film, and he was also featured in the film commenting about dams.

In 2018, in acknowledgment that sustainability and responsible practices are core to Patagonia, Yvon Chouinard was recognized with the [Sierra Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierra_Club "Sierra Club")'s top award, the [John Muir Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierra_Club_John_Muir_Award "Sierra Club John Muir Award").[[17]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-17)

In 2022, Chouinard announced that he was donating ownership in Patagonia to a [trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_(law) "Trust (law)") to ensure profits are used for addressing [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change").[[18]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-18)[[19]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-19) Chouinard's family retains control of the company's voting stock through the Patagonia Purpose Trust.[[20]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-20)[[21]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-21)

In 1971, Chouinard met and married his wife, Malinda Pennoyer, who was an art and home economics student at [California State University, Fresno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_University,_Fresno "California State University, Fresno").[[22]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-22)[[23]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-23) They have a son (Fletcher) and a daughter (Claire), both of whom work for Patagonia.[[24]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-24)

In 2008, Chouinard received an honorary doctorate from [Bowdoin College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowdoin_College "Bowdoin College").[[25]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-25) In 2021, he received an honorary degree from [Bates College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bates_College "Bates College").[[26]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard#cite_note-26)

Yvon Chouinard
==============

Y C

Yvon Chouinard is the founder and owner of Patagonia, Inc., based in Ventura, California. He began in business by designing, manufacturing, and distributing rock climbing equipment in the late 1950s. His tinkering led to an improved ice ax that is the basis for modern ice ax design. In 1964 he produced his first mail-order catalog, a one-page mimeographed sheet containing advice not to expect fast delivery during climbing season. In 2001, along with Craig Mathews, owner of West Yellowstone’s Blue Ribbon Flies, he started One Percent for the Planet, an alliance of businesses that contribute at least 1 percent of their net annual sales to groups on a list of researched and approved environmental organizations.
