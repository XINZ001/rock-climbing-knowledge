Ron Kauk

Personal information
Born September 23, 1957 (age 68)

[Redwood City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redwood_City "Redwood City"), California, United States

**Ron Kauk** (born 23 September 1957) is an American rock climber. Kauk is associated with [Camp 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camp_4_(Yosemite) "Camp 4 (Yosemite)") in [Yosemite Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yosemite_Valley "Yosemite Valley"), where he lived for decades, now a resident of [El Portal, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Portal,_California "El Portal, California").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-loosencape-1)

In 1975, he [made the first free ascent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing") of the east face of [Washington Column](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_Column "Washington Column") with [John Long](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Long_(climber) "John Long (climber)") and [John Bachar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bachar "John Bachar"). In 1978 he climbed the roof crack _[Separate Reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separate\_Reality\_(climbing\_route) "Separate Reality (climbing route)"),_ without [camming devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_loaded_camming_device "Spring loaded camming device"). The same year he put up _[Midnight Lightning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midnight\_Lightning\_(bouldering) "Midnight Lightning (bouldering)")_.[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-OUT-2) In 1979, along with [John Roskelley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Roskelley "John Roskelley"), Kim Schmitz, and Bill Forrest, he made the [first ascent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_ascent "First ascent") of _East Face_, [Uli Biaho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uli_Biaho "Uli Biaho"), Pakistan. In 1990 he put up the rap-bolted _Crossroads_ and in 1997 he climbed _Magic Line_ on pre-placed gear, both in Yosemite.

Kauk was born and raised in [Redwood City, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redwood_City,_California "Redwood City, California").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-loosencape-1)

Ron Kauk [top roping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_roping "Top roping") Bachar-Yerian route (5.11c) on Medlicott Dome, Right Side. [Tuolumne Meadows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuolumne_Meadows "Tuolumne Meadows").

In 1975, he [made the first free ascent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing") of the east face of [Washington Column](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_Column "Washington Column") with [John Long](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Long_(climber) "John Long (climber)") and [John Bachar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bachar "John Bachar"), renaming the route _Astroman_ ([5.11c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing) "Grade (climbing)")).[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-loosencape-1)[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]_Astroman_ held title as the hardest long free route in Yosemite Valley for a year.[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-3)

Also notable was _[Separate Reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separate\_Reality\_(climbing\_route) "Separate Reality (climbing route)")_ (5.12a), an intimidating roof crack which Kauk originally climbed in 1978 without [camming devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_loaded_camming_device "Spring loaded camming device").[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-4) The same year he put up _[Midnight Lightning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midnight\_Lightning\_(bouldering) "Midnight Lightning (bouldering)")_ (V8),[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-OUT-2) one of America's best known [bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering") problems because of its difficulty and convenient location in the [Camp 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camp_4_(Yosemite) "Camp 4 (Yosemite)") campground.

In the 1980s Kauk spent time in Europe visiting the [limestone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limestone "Limestone")[crags](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cliff "Cliff") and competing in [sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") competitions. He saw how European techniques such as [red pointing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_point "Red point") and bolting on [rappel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rappel "Rappel") could lead to a higher level of climbing difficulty. However, these practices were frowned upon by some Yosemite regulars. According to one account,[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-5) Bachar chopped the bolts on _Punchline_ (5.12c) which Kauk installed in 1988, causing a fight between Bachar and Mark Chapman in the Camp 4 parking lot.

Kauk went on to put up the hardest routes in Yosemite, such as the rap-bolted _Crossroads_ (5.13a) in 1990 and _Magic Line_ (5.14c) which he climbed in 1997 on pre-placed gear.[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-6) In [Tuolumne Meadows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuolumne_Meadows "Tuolumne Meadows") he rap-bolted a route that Bachar had started in a ground-up style, provocatively naming the route _Peace_ (5.13c/d).

Many of Kauk's exploits have been caught on film. In 1989 he climbed a five pitch route called _Backbone_ (5.13a) at [Smith Rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smith_Rock_State_Park "Smith Rock State Park") for [NBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NBC "NBC") Sportsworld,[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk#cite_note-7) and he repeated his toughest [first ascents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_ascents "First ascents") for Eric Perlman's _Masters of Stone_ videos. In 1992, Kauk trained and doubled [Sylvester Stallone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester_Stallone "Sylvester Stallone") along with late climber [Wolfgang Güllich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_G%C3%BCllich "Wolfgang Güllich") for [Cliffhanger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cliffhanger_(1993_film) "Cliffhanger (1993 film)"). He also doubled [Michael Rooker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Rooker "Michael Rooker"), [Leon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leon_Robinson "Leon Robinson"), and actress [Janine Turner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janine_Turner "Janine Turner"). In 1999 Kauk trained [Tom Cruise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Cruise "Tom Cruise") for the climbing scenes in _[Mission: Impossible 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mission:\_Impossible\_2 "Mission: Impossible 2")_.

In 2000 Kauk produced _Yosemite: Ascending Rhythms_, a climbing video highlighting the scenic splendor of his favorite crag.

Kauk named some of his first ascents (e.g. _Astroman_, _Midnight Lightning_) after [Jimi Hendrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix "Jimi Hendrix") songs. Books by [Carlos Castaneda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlos_Castaneda "Carlos Castaneda") are another source of names (e.g. _Separate Reality_, _Tales of Power_).

### 原文全文（英文）

```text
At the climber’s campground in Yosemite Valley, known as Camp 4, Ron was surrounded by a community of like-minded individuals seeking meaning in the vertical challenges of the granite walls – walls carved by the forces of nature. A list of Ron’s contemporaries would be a _Who’s Who_ of American rock climbing. Together, they explored the Valley and beyond, venturing into the granite domes of the high country in Tuolumne Meadows and down into the eastern Sierras. They had a blank canvas in the natural art of Yosemite, and pushed the limits of human climbing.

Ron’s career as a climber quickly reached international proportions, and he spent several years in Europe exploring its climbing areas and participating in climbing competitions. His reputation created media opportunities, including roles in the Hollywood productions _Cliffhanger_ and _Mission Impossible_. He continues to consider climbing as a way of life that furthers his education and commitment to respecting Yosemite, a place that powerfully evokes the reality of our connection to the natural world.

Education Nature’s Way
----------------------

Ron in more recent years has been working to express his appreciation for Yosemite. He has actively engaged with the [Native American community](https://vimeo.com/117978250) which rightfully has a spiritual and historical connection to Yosemite. He worked on a project to restore Fern Spring as a beautiful and unlittered location where entry into Yosemite Valley can be celebrated by communing with its pristine water. And he has spent the summers as a volunteer in the Tuolumne Meadows campground supplying firewood for ranger camp, organizing cleanups, and reminding visitors through countless conversations about the beauty of Yosemite.

During the summers at Tuolumne Meadows, Ron visits his many familiar climbs and boulders, renewing his appreciation for each and every crack, knob, and glacier-polished surface that represents the etchings of geology. He is like a scholar reading ancient manuscripts, looking for undeciphered references and hidden meanings.He smells the wind, appreciates the thunderstorms, the sun, and the flow of the cascading rivers. Our awareness of these elements is the key to our understanding of the human condition. Life and nature are great teachers and offer many paths to insight, inspiration and appreciation. For Ron, it is a simple summary: “Take care of nature, and nature will take care of you.”

Life Changes
------------

In August, 1998, a freak accident gave Ron’s life a new twist. Ron was riding his motorcycle on the main one-way loop in Yosemite Valley. Just as Bridalveil Falls was coming into view, Ron saw a car coming the wrong way. Ron tried to swerve, but it was too late. His left leg hit the car, and it was broken. Ron had never sustained a major rock climbing injury, but ironically, he now had a broken leg.

His leg in a cast, he spent some time recovering with his family and friends away from Yosemite, but as soon as he was able to get around on his own, he returned. One moonlit evening, Ron found himself at the Valley View pullout, looking at Bridalveil Falls and the silhouettes of the granite formations, all familiar to him in their intimate details from his climbing experiences. He was overcome by their beauty, and what they had done for him — struck by the kindness of nature, how it had saved him from his youthful predicament, and what his life as a climber in Yosemite had taught him. Tears welled from the emotions of gratitude and appreciation. A clarity of commitment emerged, how he could share the kindness of nature with others.

It struck Ron that the kindness was expressed in so many ways — the way in which the huge granite formations were formed so patiently over time, carved and polished in infinite variations, all of which he appreciated through his climbing; the annual cycle of snow, rain, and runoffs that supply the beautiful rivers and springs; the generosity of Fern Spring that graces the entrance into Yosemite Valley by providing a steady flow of the purest of water; the flora and fauna that thrive in this environment and respond to the annual cycles in majestic forms (giant sequoias and the bears) as well as the delicate (the wildflowers and the tiny frogs). The water that runs out of the tap in San Francisco is a precious symbol of this kindness, for without that, we might be drinking water created by the carbon-intensive evaporation of sea water in desalination plants littering the California coast. Ron, beginning as a young man downstream from the Hetch Hetchy, has figuratively swum upstream to the water source, and through his life as a climber, come into contact with the realities of nature.
```

[Ron Kauk](http://www.patagonia.com/us/patagonia.go?assetid=1878) is one of the best known climbers in [Yosemite Valley](http://www.nps.gov/yose/planyourvisit/yv.htm). He was among that early generation of big wall pioneers that set the standards and established the major routes that are still popular today. Since 1974 he’s been a regular in the Yosemite climbing community. Having made the first free ascent of [Washington Column](http://www.supertopo.com/rock-climbing/Yosemite-Valley-Washington-Column-South-Face) in 1975 with [John Long](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Long_%28climber%29) and [John Bachar](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bachar) Kauk renamed the route [Astroman](http://www.supertopo.com/rock-climbing/Yosemite-Valley-Washington-Column-Astroman) and it became the most challenging climb in the Valley for more than 10 years.

 On a joy trip out to the west coast this summer I had the chance to meet Kauk and talk to him about his younger days in Yosemite.

 “For me it was like entering this incredible world of granite and rivers and trees and this interesting like-minded group of people in this place we’re sitting in which is camp four,” Kauk said. “It was colorful and energized with this enthusiasm to climb but also live simply. To think that you could walk in and feel so comfortable and belong to this group and inspire each other just out here on these boulders … or out there on those big walls once you learned the fundamentals.”

 It’s been more than 30 years and Kauk is still climbing in Yosemite. With a life philosophy that includes a deep and abiding appreciation for nature he continues to inspire young people through a non-profit called [Sacred Rok](http://sacredrok.org/). Making the connection between spirituality and the preservation of wild places he’s taking the sport of rock climbing to a whole new level.
