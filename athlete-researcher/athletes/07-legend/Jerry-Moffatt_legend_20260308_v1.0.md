**Jerry Moffatt** (born 18 March 1963) is a British [rock climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_climbing "Rock climbing") and climbing author who is widely considered as being the best British rock climber from the early-1980s to the early-1990s, and was arguably the best rock climber in the world in the mid-1980s, and an important climber in the [history of the sport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_rock_climbing "History of rock climbing").

As a [sport climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing"), Moffatt was one of the [first climbers in history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Onsighted_/_Flashed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)") to [onsight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onsight "Onsight") routes of grade [7b+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.12c), [7c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.12d), and [7c+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13a), and also the [first in history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Redpointed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)") to climb routes of grade [8a+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13c), and probably [8c+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14c).[[c]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-Hubble-9) As a [competition climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_competition "Climbing competition"), Moffatt won several of the nascent tour events, and retired ranked first in the world. As a [boulder climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering"), Moffatt was one of the [first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Solved_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)") to solve problems of boulder grade [8A+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Bouldering "Grade (climbing)")(V12), and [8B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Bouldering "Grade (climbing)")(V13). As a [traditional climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing"), Moffatt established some of the most intimidating routes at the time in Britain, which are still rarely repeated, and in particular, the _[Master's Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master%27s\_Wall "Master's Wall")_ (E7 6b) in 1983.[[a]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-MW-1)

Moffatt was noted for the intensity of his training, and the co-development of training techniques now considered mainstream in professional climbing. In 1991, Moffatt opened Britain's first indoor [climbing centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_wall "Climbing wall"), "The Foundry" in [Sheffield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheffield "Sheffield"). He has written books on mental preparation for competition climbing.

On leaving school in 1980, aged 17, Moffatt began to immediately attract attention in the British rock climbing media with repeat ascents of some of the hardest British [traditional climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing") routes, most notably [Ron Fawcett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Fawcett "Ron Fawcett")'s _Strawberries_ (E7 6b).[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3)[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GYM-10) Moffatt described Fawcett as a "massive legend", but that "by 1983, I was pretty sure I was climbing better than him in terms of difficulty. I looked up to him though and we never had any problems".[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) Aged 18, and living in an abandoned shed at [Stoney Middleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoney_Middleton "Stoney Middleton") crag, Moffatt started to establish his own test-pieces such as _Little Plum_ (E6 6c, in 1981–82),[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) one of the hardest British [sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") routes that had repelled all attempts to free it, and one of the first British [7c+/8a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)") sport routes.[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GYM-10)[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-UKC8-11)

In 1982, aged 19, Moffatt travelled to the United States, where he repeated America's hardest routes, and did the [world's first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Onsighted_/_Flashed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)")[flash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flash_(climbing) "Flash (climbing)") of a [7b+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.12c) with _Super Crack_ in [Shawangunks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shawangunks "Shawangunks"), and _Equinox_ in [Joshua Tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joshua_Tree_National_Park "Joshua Tree National Park").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) In 1983, on returning home, Moffatt established his most dangerous traditional climbing route, _[Master's Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master%27s\_Wall "Master's Wall")_[[d]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-12) (E7 6b)[[a]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-MW-1) at [Clogwyn Du'r Arddu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clogwyn_Du%27r_Arddu "Clogwyn Du'r Arddu").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) In a later interview, Moffatt said, "At that time to be respected, you really had to be putting up really scary new routes. That was where it was at, in Britain at least. Master's Wall is probably where I risked most".[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) Later that summer, Moffatt focused on sport climbing, establishing _Masterclass_ at Pen Trwyn, the first British [8a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13b) route.[[e]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-16) In September 1983, Moffatt travelled to Germany where he did the [world's second-ever onsight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Onsighted_/_Flashed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)") of a [7c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.12d) with _Heisse Finger_, and then went on to climb the [world's first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Single-pitch_routes "List of first ascents (sport climbing)") sport climb at [8a+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13c), _The Face_, in [Altmühl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altm%C3%BChl_Valley_Nature_Park "Altmühl Valley Nature Park").[[11]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PMEVOL-15)

In 1984, on his 21st birthday, Moffatt flashed _Chimpanzodrome_ at Saussois, and then did the [world's first onsight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Onsighted_/_Flashed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)") of a [7c+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13a) with _Pol Pot_ at [Verdon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verdon_Gorge "Verdon Gorge").[[11]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PMEVOL-15) Later in 1984, Moffatt freed _Revelations_ at Ravens Tor in the [Peak District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peak_District "Peak District"), Britain's first [8a+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13c) route.[[b]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-RE-2)[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) In June 1985, Moffatt did the first free ascent of the Yosemite's [Lost Arrow Spire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lost_Arrow_Spire "Lost Arrow Spire") with [Ron Kauk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Kauk "Ron Kauk"), as a live television event with an audience of over 30 million for [ABC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company "American Broadcasting Company")'s _[Wide World of Sports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wide\_World\_of\_Sports\_(American\_TV\_program) "Wide World of Sports (American TV program)")_.[[12]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-17) During 1985 to late 1986, Moffatt was largely out with a [compressed nerve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nerve_compression_syndrome "Nerve compression syndrome") injury (initially mis-diagnosed as [tendonitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tendonitis "Tendonitis")), that required surgery and rehabilitation.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3)

On returning in 1987, Moffatt spent the next two years repeating the three hardest [8b+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14a) routes in France (_Le Rage de Vivre_, _Le Minumum_, _La Spectre des Surmutant_ in [Buoux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buoux "Buoux")), and in the United States (_Scarface_, _White Wedding_, _To Bolt Or Not To Be_ in [Smith Rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smith_Rock_State_Park "Smith Rock State Park")), while also creating his own 8b+ route, _Stone Love_ in the Frankenjura in Germany.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) In May 1990, he returned to Pen Trwyn in Wales to create _Liquid Ambar_, Britain's first [8c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14b) route, and which has been regraded to [8c+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14c), which would make it the [world's first](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Redpointed_by_men "List of first ascents (sport climbing)") 8c+.[[c]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-Hubble-9)[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-UKC8-11)[[14]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-19) Moffatt continued to travel widely, repeating _Punks In The Gym_[8b+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14a) in 1992 in Australia, establishing Canada's first [8b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13d), _The Big Kahuna_, in 1993 in Ontario, and freeing routes such as _Evolution_[8c+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.14c) in 1995 at Ravens Tor.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3)

In 1989 and 1990, Moffatt concentrated on the emerging sport of [competition climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_competition "Climbing competition"), winning ten international events over two years, and never finishing worse than fifth in any competition he entered.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) He won the first-ever [UIAA Climbing World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UIAA_Climbing_World_Cup "UIAA Climbing World Cup") Grand Prix event that was held in 1989 in [Leeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leeds "Leeds"),[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GYM-10)[[15]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-20) and finished third overall,[[f]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-22) in the inaugural 1989 [IFSC Climbing World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IFSC_Climbing_World_Cup "IFSC Climbing World Cup") that concluded in [Lyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyon "Lyon").[[16]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GUARD-21) In a 2009 interview, Moffatt noted that his initial competition form was poor until he began to study the mental side of competing, saying, "I found out how your mind works under pressure and immediately after reading that book I competed in Leeds and won my first competition".[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) The book that Moffatt was referring to was _With Winning in Mind_ (1988), by American Olympian, [Lanny Bassham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanny_Bassham "Lanny Bassham").[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-CP-7)[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GYM-10)

In September 1990, aged 27, Moffatt retired from competition climbing and at that time was rated first on the Association of Sport Climbers International (ASCI) rankings.[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GYM-10)[[17]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-23)[[18]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-8anu2-24) On retiring, he was voted most prolific competition climber 1980s in the French climbing magazine _Vertical_.[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-REV-5)[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GYM-10) In his 2009 autobiography he said of his retirement, "I no longer had energy the energy to keep it all up. I wanted to get myself back again. I wanted to see my friends. I wanted to climb for myself. I wanted to do first ascents. Most of all I wanted to have fun".[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GYM-10)[[18]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-8anu2-24)

Moffatt had been an early adopter of bouldering from the outset of his career, saying in a 2009 interview, "My early boulder problems weren't publicized, no one seemed to be interested in them at all".[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) In 1988, he solved _Superman_ at [Cressbrook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cressbrook "Cressbrook"), one of the [first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Boulder_problems "List of first ascents (sport climbing)")[8A+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Bouldering "Grade (climbing)")(V12) boulder problems in the world.[[11]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PMEVOL-15) In 1991, Moffatt traveled to Yosemite and at [Camp 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camp_4_(Yosemite) "Camp 4 (Yosemite)") solved _Sick It_ at [8A+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Bouldering "Grade (climbing)")(V12), and _The Force_ at [8A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Bouldering "Grade (climbing)")(V11), two of the hardest in America at that time.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) He returned to Yosemite in 1993, and added _The Dominator_ at [8B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Bouldering "Grade (climbing)")(V13), considered one of the [first-ever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_first_ascents_(sport_climbing)#Boulder_problems "List of first ascents (sport climbing)")[8B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Bouldering "Grade (climbing)")(V13) boulders ever climbed.[[11]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PMEVOL-15) In 1997, he climbed the 12-metre high _Samson_ (E8 7b) at Burbage South, while technically the hardest [gritstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gritstone "Gritstone") route at that time, with [boulder pads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering_mat "Bouldering mat") is now a [highball bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering#Highball_bouldering "Bouldering") problem at [8a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.13b).[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) In 2002, on Stanage Edge in the Peak District Moffatt solved the long-term boulder problem _The Ace_ at [8B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Bouldering "Grade (climbing)")(V13), the hardest boulder problem in Britain at the time, and effectively Moffat's last major new route.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3)

In 1996, Moffatt and climbing partner [Ben Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Moon_(rock_climber) "Ben Moon (rock climber)"), featured in a film by Simon Tucker called _The Real Thing_, which is considered an important early film in the bouldering film genre, and featured the novel training techniques they were using (i.e. [campus board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board "Campus board")-type installations) that are now mainstream.[[19]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-25) The pair would feature in other bouldering films, including _Stick It_ (2001), and _Stone Love_ (2001).[[20]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-26)

Moffatt is also known for his [free soloing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_soloing "Free soloing") of challenging British traditional climbing routes, telling _[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Guardian "The Guardian")_ in 2011, "You have to have an insane confidence in your own ability to go soloing".[[21]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-Guardian-27) In 1983, Moffatt was particularly active in pushing his limits, soloing _Scarab_ (E6 6b) at Stoney Middleton, _Right Wall_ (E5 6a) at [Dinas Cromlech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinas_Cromlech "Dinas Cromlech"), and days before he climbed _Master's Wall_ at Clogwyn Du'r Arddu, free soloing the neighboring _Great Wall_ (E4 6a), _The Boldest_ (E4 5c), and _Curving Arete_ (E4 5c).[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-REV-5) However, the death of his friend Neil Molnar, who died soloing that year, saw Moffatt scale back his soloing.[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-REV-5)

Moffatt's 2009 autobiography, _Revelations_, written with co-author Niall Grimes,[[22]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-CL5-28) won the Grand Prize at the 2009 [Banff Mountain Book Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banff_Mountain_Book_Festival "Banff Mountain Book Festival"), and was shortlisted for the 2009 [Boardman Tasker Prize for Mountain Literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boardman_Tasker_Prize_for_Mountain_Literature "Boardman Tasker Prize for Mountain Literature").[[23]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-29) In 2015, it was listed in _[Climbing magazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing\_(magazine) "Climbing (magazine)")'s_ "33 Must Read Climbing Books", saying, "If you ever want to be inspired to train, just read any chapter. What a hardman!".[[24]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-30)

In 2017, Moffatt completed his second book, _Mastermind: Mental training for climbers_, focused on sports psychology for sport climbers, and built on the methods Lanny Bassham outlined (earlier).[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GYM-10)[[25]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-31)

In 1991, Moffatt and Paul Reeve opened The Foundry Climbing Centre ("the Foundry"), in Sheffield, and was the first of its type in the UK with leading, top-roping, bouldering, and interchangeable holds.[[26]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-WC-32) They were originally financially supported by [Wild Country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_Country_(company) "Wild Country (company)"), which owned 51%, but Moffatt and Reeve later bought out Wild Country's share, and now own it equally.[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-CP-7)[[26]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-WC-32)

Moffatt is widely considered to be the best British rock climber from the early-1980s to the early-1990s and was arguably the best rock climber in the world during the mid to late 1980s. In 2006, the _[BBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC "BBC")_ said: "Jerry is one of, if not the greatest climbers of all time, and his name is known and highly respected in the global world of rock climbing".[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-BBC-8) In 2009, _PlanetMountain_ called him "one of the most successful climbers in the world during the 1980s and 1990s", and "Jerry Moffatt is, in short, a climber who like few others left his lasting mark, worldwide, on the early development of this vertical game".[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) In 2009, Jens Larssen of climbing website _8a.nu_ said "Jerry Moffat was probably the best climber in the world during the 1980s",[[18]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-8anu2-24) while _[Climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing\_(magazine) "Climbing (magazine)")_ magazine called him a "world rock climbing legend".[[22]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-CL5-28) In 2011, _The Guardian_ said of him, "That's Jerry Moffatt, once the best rock climber in the world".[[21]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-Guardian-27) In 2018, _Gripped_ said, "Moffatt was the best climber of the 1980s and early 1990s".[[27]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GR7-33)

In a British context, Moffatt (and contemporaries such as [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Moon_(climber) "Ben Moon (climber)") and [Dawes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnny_Dawes "Johnny Dawes")) carried on the legacy of [Peter Livesey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Livesey "Peter Livesey") and Ron Fawcett.[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-BBC-8) However, Moffatt (and Moon) soon reached the limits of what they could safely achieve with traditional climbing, and instead followed the continental European trend into bolted sport climbing,[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3) and competition climbing.[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-BBC-8) Moffatt, and Moon, were also part of a new group of climbers, that included Germans [Wolfgang Gullich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Gullich "Wolfgang Gullich"), [Kurt Albert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Albert "Kurt Albert"), and [Stefan Glowacz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stefan_Glowacz "Stefan Glowacz"),[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-PM1-3)[[27]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GR7-33) who were using new training techniques (e.g. [campus board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_board "Campus board"), [plyometrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plyometrics "Plyometrics")), and embracing [bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering"), to materially improve their technique and the standard of routes they could climb.[[27]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-GR7-33)[[28]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-34) In 2006, Moffatt told the _BBC_, "When I started climbing I wanted to do dangerous climbs, in my mid-20's I wanted to do really hard climbs with ropes, and then when I got to my late-30's it got shorter and I got more obsessed with trying to do the hardest move I could possibly do... It might be just one or two moves".[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-BBC-8)

Moffatt is married to his wife Sharon with whom he has two children.[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-REV-5)[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-CP-7) Outside of climbing, he is an avid surfer, a sport he took up while injured on a 1993 climbing trip to Yosemite[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-REV-5)[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-CP-7)

Moffatt was diagnosed with [dyslexia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia") at school,[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-UKC8-11)[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-CP-7) which led to his parents sending him to [St David's College, Llandudno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St_David%27s_College,_Llandudno "St David's College, Llandudno"), a boarding school with a special programme for dyslexic children.[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-REV-5)

In April 1987, his younger brother Toby died aged 21 in the United States (in his autobiography, Moffatt says that he named his climb _Liquid Ambar_ after the tree [Liquidambar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquidambar "Liquidambar") – although Moffatt spells it as two words[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-MW-4) – in memory of Toby, who was a keen gardener and wished to plant such a tree on his return to England).[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-REV-5)[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerry_Moffatt#cite_note-CP-7)

[Jerry Moffatt](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news?search_opt=checked&parola=Jerry%20Moffatt) is certainly one of the legendary figures of modern climbing. After serving an outstanding "apprenticeship" with British trad, throughout the 80's and 90’s Moffatt left his mark on sports climbs and boulders worldwide, and unsurprisingly he was also one of the world’s first professional climbers. For a while he lived with [Wolfgang Güllich](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news?search_opt=checked&parola=Wolfgang%20G%C3%BCllich) and [Kurt Albert](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news/climbing/kurt-albert-is-dead-goodbye-to-a-climbing-legend.html), another two other undisputed greats of the "new" wave of climbing and during his stay in Germany he became involved in the development of the Campus Board, famously used by Güllich in his quest to free undisputed masterpiece: the 9a _[Action Directe](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news?search\_opt=checked&parola=Action%20Directe)_ in the Frankenjura. 

 Over the years this training device has played an essential aspect for hardcore climbing training. In this video interview Moffatt takes a step back in time to talk retrace the birth of the Campus Board, discuss early training methods and highlight the importance of quality vs quantity when training pure power. The interview also includes early footage of his training, together with [Ben Moon](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news?search_opt=checked&parola=Ben%20Moon), in their cellar in Sheffield.

Jerry Moffatt is, handily, one of modern climbing’s great innovators. In the 1980s and 1990s, [Moffatt and hard-climbing cronies](https://linode.explorersweb.com/exwebs-links-of-the-week-8/) like Ben Moon put up some of the UK’s burliest boulder problems and routes. They did what many had thought impossible. But to do what had never been done before, they had to develop a method that had not previously existed.

“For me, the most important thing is variety and changing where you climb,” Moffatt states. And thus systematic training for climbing was born.

Moffatt’s approach — to train multiple styles in a variety of environments from bouldering gyms to gritstone — was simple in theory but immense in application. Not only would the great experiment demand time, testing, and physical dedication, but many of their contemporaries would balk at the idea. Train in a bouldering gym to climb better outside?

“Everyone thought I was crazy,” he chuckles. But it soon became apparent that his visionary approach to training would bear substantial results.

Some 30 years on from his psych-fueled heyday, Moffatt continues to train and send as hard as ever. Spend some time learning from one of the sport’s unparalleled masters in this short but delightful retrospective.
