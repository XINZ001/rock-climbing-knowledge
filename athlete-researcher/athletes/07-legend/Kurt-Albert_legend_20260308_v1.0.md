**Kurt Albert** (January 28, 1954 – September 28, 2010) was a German [climber](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing "Climbing") and photographer. He started climbing at the age of 14. Before he committed himself to a career of climbing in 1986, he was a mathematics and physics teacher.

Climbing career
---------------

At the age of seventeen, he climbed the Walker Spur in the [Grandes Jorasses](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grandes_Jorasses "Grandes Jorasses"), and one year later he climbed the [north face of the Eiger](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eiger_north_face#Nordwand "Eiger north face"). After a visit to the [Saxon Switzerland](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saxon_Switzerland "Saxon Switzerland") climbing area in Saxony, Germany in 1973 he recognized the potential of [free climbing](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing"). He started to free climb in his home climbing area, the [Frankenjura](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankenjura "Frankenjura"). In the routes, he would now try to ascend while free climbing, he would—in between attempts—paint a red 'X' on the rocks near pitons he did not need as holds or steps. Once he could place a red 'X' on all the pitons and hooks in the route, and was thus able to [free climb](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing") the entire route, he would paint a red dot at the base of the route. From this comes the English term "[redpoint](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing) "Redpoint (climbing)")", which is derived from the German _Rotpunkt_, meaning "redpoint". In many ways, this was the origin of the free climbing movement that led to the development of [sport climbing](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") some years later.

Death
-----

Albert was severely injured in a climbing accident on September 26, 2010. He fell 18 metres (59 ft) while taking pictures at [via ferrata](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Via_ferrata "Via ferrata")_Höhenglücksteig_ (near [Hirschbach, Bavaria](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hirschbach,_Bavaria "Hirschbach, Bavaria")) and died two days later while hospitalised in [Erlangen](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erlangen "Erlangen").[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Albert#cite_note-1)[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Albert#cite_note-2)

Kurt Albert obituary
====================

This article is more than 15 years old

Innovative pioneer of free climbing and inventor of the redpoint system

[Ed Douglas](https://www.theguardian.com/profile/eddouglas)

Mon 18 Oct 2010 13.46 EDT

[Prefer the Guardian on Google](https://www.google.com/preferences/source?q=theguardian.com)

Even among great athletes, few can claim to have revolutionised their sport. Yet the climber Kurt Albert, who has died in a fall aged 56, did so twice, first in his native [Germany](https://www.theguardian.com/world/germany), where he helped develop what has become known as sport climbing, and then on the biggest cliffs around the world, from Patagonia to the Himalaya, and from the jungles of Venezuela to the wastes of Baffin Island in the Arctic.

His powerfully muscular frame, topped by a mop of blond hair and his trademark moustache, was a familiar sight in climbing magazines for 35 years, but even at the height of his fame, which in [Europe](https://www.theguardian.com/world/europe-news) was considerable, he remained himself: approachable, good-humoured and endlessly enthusiastic.

His affable personality, however, hid an extraordinary drive and toughness, as well as an innovative mind. The British rock climbing star Jerry Moffatt, a close friend, described Albert as being completely reliable: "If you were in a jam, he was the man you'd want there."

Albert was born in the Bavarian city of Nuremberg, where his adventurous streak was quickly evident. As a boy, living on the eighth floor of a tower block, he would climb out of his window and along the outside of the building to visit his best friend. Nor was he scared of authority. Raised as a Catholic, he cheeked his local priest once too often, and regaled friends with the story of how the priest chased him down the street.

Nuremberg is close to the beautiful forests of the Frankenjura, whose steep limestone crags now hold some of the most difficult rock climbs in the world. In 1968, however, when Albert started climbing, these cliffs were used purely as practice for the Alps. Climbers would hang off pitons rather than their fingers.

Albert too followed this practice, as he took on the usual apprenticeship to becoming a mountaineer. And he was good at it, climbing the north face of the Eiger and other hard alpine climbs. But in 1973 he went to the East German climbing centre of Elbsandstein, near Dresden, a fantastical collection of sandstone towers then behind the Iron Curtain. At Elbsandstein, hidden from western climbers, a different ethic prevailed, one whose purity affected Albert deeply.

In the old East Germany he made lifelong friends such as Bernd Arnold, who used his own strength and agility to climb routes free of all the ironmongery that aided Albert back home. That process of freeing routes was getting underway in America and Britain, but Albert took it back to the Frankenjura. Once he had climbed an old aid route free, Albert needed a way of telling climbers that it could be done. The previous generation had marked climbs with a red circle at the bottom, and so Kurt decided to fill these in with red paint, so others would know that the climb could now be done in this new style. It was called "rotpunkt" or red point, and redpointing quickly spread around the world as a way to push free climbing standards around the world.

He was always a great innovator, creating training techniques where there were none, building a gym in the cellar of his apartment, which he shared for 11 years with the younger and equally famous Wolfgang Güllich. Their flat became a meeting-house for climbers from all over the world, testament to the ability of both men to make friends wherever they went.

Over the next few years, Albert climbed hundreds of new routes of a harder and harder standard, culminating in Sautanz at Grossweinsteiner, a new benchmark in German climbing. He gave up his job as a maths and physics teacher – he won Rubik's Cube contests too – and became a professional climber when such people were rare. In 1984 he was awarded the Silbernes Lorbeerblatt, Germany's highest sports award.

Albert, however, had barely got going. Along with his technical skill, he had a passion for adventure and travel, especially for the huge landscapes of the US where he kept a jeep for his frequent visits. He wondered whether he could transfer the free climbing skills he had developed in Germany to the huge big walls still being climbed in the Alps and beyond with the old aid-climbing methods.

He started in the Italian Dolomites, free climbing with Gerold Sprachmann two of the most famous routes there, the Hasse-Brandler on the Cima Grande and the even harder Swiss Route on the Cima Ovest, which proved as difficult as Sautanz, but on a cliff 600m high.

If you could free climb big walls like this in the Alps, he decided, anything was possible, and he set out to prove it. With Güllich and others he travelled to Pakistan in 1988 to free climb an existing route on the Nameless Tower above the Baltoro glacier, managing to replicate their approach at sea level on a wall that reaches a lung-bursting 6,242m – more than 20,000ft.

This was groundbreaking stuff, inspiring climbers all around the world, including themselves. Güllich and Albert were back the following year on the Nameless Tower, climbing an entirely new route, almost all of it free and at an incredibly high standard of difficulty. It showed Albert at his best, leading and fighting through to the top after Güllich was injured. They called their route Eternal Flame.

Albert followed this with a sequence of similar projects all over the world, from Antarctica to northern Canada and Greenland and everywhere in between. Perhaps his best climb, however, came on the huge east pillar of FitzRoy in Patagonia in 1995, with 44 rope lengths of the highest difficulty. By then, Güllich was dead, killed in a car crash, a loss that hurt Albert badly.

His enthusiasm for the sport, however, remained undiminished. In the last few years of his life he honed his ethical approach still further, refusing to use porters or machines to approach mountains, walking for 400km across Baffin Island with all his gear to climb the Polar Bear Spire. Albert's sudden death, falling from a "via ferrata" – a usually safe system of ladders that allows non-climbers to experience steep cliffs – was tragically ironic for someone who had survived the harshest conditions.

Kurt Albert, climber, born 28 January 1954; died 28 September 2010

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[Kurt Albert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Albert "Kurt Albert") climbing at the "Streitberger Schild" near Streitberg, [Frankenjura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankenjura "Frankenjura"), Germany, the place where he made the first _Rotpunkt_ in 1975.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-UKC-1)

In [rock climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_climbing "Rock climbing"), a **redpoint** is a successful [free-climb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing") of a climbing route performed by a [lead climber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead_climbing "Lead climbing").

The climber can use rock-climbing equipment only for protection, not as an artificial aid to rest or climb. The climber can have attempted or practised the route many times beforehand, such as by [headpointing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headpoint "Headpoint") or by [top roping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_roping "Top roping").[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-Gripped-2)

When performed on the first attempt, a successful redpoint is typically called [onsight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onsight "Onsight") (without prior knowledge about [beta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_(climbing) "Beta (climbing)")), or [flash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flash_(rock_climbing) "Flash (rock climbing)") (with beta provided by others).[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-Berry1-3) Typically, climbers try to redpoint a route after having failed to onsight or flash it.

Regardless of the number of attempts required to perform it, the first successful redpoint of a climbing route is recorded as the [first free ascent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_free_ascent "First free ascent") (FFA) of that route.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-C-4)

In contrast to an onsight or flash attempt, for a redpoint ascent it is not relevant whether the climber had previously failed on the route, or practiced any of the individual movements required to climb it. It is also acceptable to undertake prior inspections of the route to learn its [beta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_(climbing) "Beta (climbing)"), for example by [abseiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abseil "Abseil") down the route.[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-CN1-5)

If the lead climber falls during an attempted redpoint ascent and thus ends up in a position of hanging from their rope so that bodyweight is being held — even partly — by the rope, then they must return to the very bottom of the route, pull their rope free of the route (e.g. free from any climbing protection), and completely re-start their redpoint ascent from scratch. Pulling the rope free before re-starting is also known as "climbing a route clean" but this should not be confused with the broader topic of [clean climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clean_climbing "Clean climbing").[[5]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-CN1-5)[[3]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-Berry1-3)

Climbers can pause and rest during a redpoint ascent but they cannot use the rope—or any other artificial aids—to hold their bodyweight while they rest. Unaided resting techniques such as the [kneebar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_climbing_terms#knee_bar "Glossary of climbing terms") and the [bat hang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_climbing_terms#bat_hang "Glossary of climbing terms") are allowed.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-C-4)[Hangdogging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hangdog "Hangdog") is where the climber rests on the rope after falling but then restarts climbing without returning to the ground—this is not allowed for a redpoint.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-C-4) The first climber to redpoint a route, in the absence of any prior onsight or flash of a route, has made the [first free ascent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_free_ascent "First free ascent") (FFA) of that route.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-C-4)

Where [quickdraws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quickdraws "Quickdraws") are pre-placed into the [fixed protection bolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock-climbing_equipment#Fixed "Rock-climbing equipment") on [sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing") routes, so that climber is just clipping the rope into the quickdraws as they ascend, it is called a "[pinkpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinkpoint "Pinkpoint")" — in practice, most ascents of extreme sport climbing routes are done as pinkpoints, as are ascents in modern [competition climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_climbing "Competition climbing"), so the term "pinkpoint" is no longer used in these instances.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-C-4)[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-Gripped-2)[[6]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-6) However, due to additional significant challenge of placing [climbing protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climbing_protection "Climbing protection") while ascending [traditional climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing") routes, traditional climbers continue to differentiate on whether their first free ascent was a pinkpoint (e.g. as Swiss traditional climber [Didier Berthod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Didier_Berthod "Didier Berthod") did on making the first ascent of _The Crack of Destiny_ in 2023) or a full redpoint.[[7]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-7)

The unlimited practicing that is allowed before making a redpoint ascent contrasts with the historical aversion to "[headpointing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headpoint "Headpoint")" (i.e. practicing the route on a [toprope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_roping "Top roping") beforehand) in [traditional climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing"). In the early 1980s, redpointing was therefore a term largely exclusive to [sport climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_climbing "Sport climbing").[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-Gripped-2)[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-C-4) While headpointing was then considered a _lesser form_ of first free ascent in traditional climbing (and an FFA that was headpointed would be asterisked as such),[[8]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-8) leading traditional climbers eventually followed the redpointing practices of the sport climbers, and by the 2000s, had largely dispensed with the stigma associated with headpointing.[[9]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-9)

From about the 2010s, traditional climbers were using the derived term "[greenpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenpoint_(climbing) "Greenpoint (climbing)")" (or the _Grünpunkt_, as a play on the _Rotpunkt_), to describe climbing a pre-bolted sport-climb, but only using "traditional protection" (i.e. climbing protection that is not permanently fixed via pre-placed bolts or pitons); as with redpointing, the climber may have repeatedly practiced falling on the “traditional protection” before making their greenpoint ascent.[[10]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-10)[[11]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-11) Notable examples include Austrian climber [Beat Kammerlander](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Beat_Kammerlander&action=edit&redlink=1 "Beat Kammerlander (page does not exist)")[[de](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beat_Kammerlander "de:Beat Kammerlander")]'s greenpoint of _[Prinzip Hoffnung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prinzip\_Hoffnung "Prinzip Hoffnung")_ (5.14a R, 2009) in [Bürs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B%C3%BCrs "Bürs") in Austria, and Canadian [Sonnie Trotter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonnie_Trotter "Sonnie Trotter")'s greenpoint of _The Path_ (5.14a R, 2007) in [Lake Louise, Alberta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Louise,_Alberta "Lake Louise, Alberta"),[[12]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-12)[[13]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-13) and of _East Face_ (Monkey Face) (5.13d R, 2004) at [Smith Rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smith_Rock_State_Park "Smith Rock State Park").[[14]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-14)[[15]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-15)

Repeatedly trying to make a redpoint ascent of a route can take place over any length of time, from hours to years (i.e. any time, once the initial onsight or flash attempt has failed). Climbers use the term _projecting_ to denote a longer-term _project_ to complete the FFA, or their own personal first ascent, of a route that is at the limit of their abilities.[[16]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-16)[[17]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-17) The redpoint FFA of many of contemporary sport climbing routes, particularly those that involved breaking new [grade milestones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_grade_milestones_in_rock_climbing "List of grade milestones in rock climbing"), took years, and even decades, to project (e.g. _[Realization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Realization\_(climb) "Realization (climb)")_, _[La Dura Dura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La\_Dura\_Dura "La Dura Dura")_, and _[Jumbo Love](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jumbo\_Love "Jumbo Love")_).

The term projecting is most commonly used in [bouldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouldering "Bouldering"), where in addition, the completion of a bouldering route is rarely called a 'redpoint' but instead the boulderer is usually deemed to have 'solved' the route in the manner of it being akin to a short technical problem.[[18]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-18)

The English term "redpoint" is a [loan translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loan_translation "Loan translation") of the German _Rotpunkt_ that was coined by [Kurt Albert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Albert "Kurt Albert") in the mid-1970s at [Frankenjura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankenjura "Frankenjura"). Albert would paint a red "X" on any fixed metal [pitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piton "Piton") on a rock climbing route so that he could avoid using them while climbing, thus not using any artificial aid. Once Albert was able to [free-climb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_climbing "Free climbing") the entire route, and avoid all the red "X"s, he would then paint a red "dot" (the "Roter Punkt") at the base of the route. His first _Rotpunkt_ was the [aid climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aid_climbing "Aid climbing") route _Adolf-Rott-Gedächtnis-Weg_ (V+/A1) at the Streitberger Schild crag in the Frankenjura, which he freed at [6a+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.10b) in 1975.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-UKC-1) Albert got the idea for the "red dot" from the logo and name of a brand of German coffee and kettle maker.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-UKC-1) To achieve a _Rotpunkt_, Albert additionally defined that if a climber fell during the ascent, they had to return to the base, pull the rope free, and re-start the climb from scratch (i.e. as if the climber had only just approached it).[[19]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-PM1-19)[[20]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-PM2-20)

The connotation spread of a "redpoint" being a route that had to be repeatedly attempted because it was so hard – which is why metal pitons had been hammered into the rock as an aid in the first place – until it could be climbed in one clean push (i.e. no falls, and any falls required a full re-start), and without any artificial aids.[[4]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-C-4)[[2]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-Gripped-2) Because these routes were already established [aid climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aid_climbing "Aid climbing") routes, Albert could not remove the pitons (that would happen in later decades), however, his _Rotpunkt_ laid down a mark to other climbers that the route could be free climbed without the use of the metal aids, and thus became an important moment in the development of free climbing.[[1]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-UKC-1) Eventually, Albert's _Rotpunkte_ became associated with the development of sport climbing in the 1980s, as many of these aids were on routes that had no possibility of even natural traditional climbing protection (e.g. no cracks), and thus bolts would be needed for protection (but not aid).[[19]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-PM1-19)[[20]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-PM2-20)

Notable redpointed climbs are chronicled by the climbing media to track progress in rock climbing standards and levels of technical difficulty; in contrast, the hardest [traditional climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_climbing "Traditional climbing") routes tend to be of lower technical difficulty due to the additional burden of having to place protection during the course of the climb, and due to the lack of any possibility of using natural protection on extreme sport climbs.[[21]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-eMontana-21)

As of January 2026, the [world's hardest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_grade_milestones_in_rock_climbing#Redpointed_by_men "List of grade milestones in rock climbing") redpointed routes are _[Silence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silence\_(climb) "Silence (climb)")_ by [Adam Ondra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Ondra "Adam Ondra"), _[DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA\_(climb) "DNA (climb)")_ by [Seb Bouin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seb_Bouin "Seb Bouin"), _B.I.G._ by [Jakob Schubert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakob_Schubert "Jakob Schubert"), and _Duality of Man_ by [Sean Bailey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sean_Bailey_(climber) "Sean Bailey (climber)") which are all at a proposed grade of [9c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.15d), however, none of these new routes have been repeated.[[22]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-22) As of June 2025, [one female](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_grade_milestones_in_rock_climbing#Redpointed_by_women "List of grade milestones in rock climbing") climber, [Brooke Raboutou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooke_Raboutou "Brooke Raboutou"), has redpointed an established route just one grade point lower at [9b+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(climbing)#Free_climbing "Grade (climbing)")(5.15c), with her 2025 ascent of _Excalibur_.[[23]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-23)[[24]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redpoint_(climbing)#cite_note-24)
