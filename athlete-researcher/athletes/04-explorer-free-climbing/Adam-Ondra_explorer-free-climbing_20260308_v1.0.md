The Italian website Planet Mountain has published an excellent interview with new details from Adam Ondra about _Change_, a likely candidate for the world’s first 9b+ (5.15c). Highlights include:

_Working the route_: Ondra put three weeks into the climb this summer, and another two weeks in the fall, the longest he had ever worked a route.

_Grade_: After he sent the first crux and felt “fresh” on the second section’s crux passage while working the climb, Ondra thought the route might be “only” 9b. [He has climbed at least five 9b (5.15b) routes before.] “But in the end I kept falling off that first section so often, it became such a demanding process both physically and mentally that I believe it is significantly harder than all the other 9b’s I’ve done,” he said.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

**Q: After so many days and months trying and training, did the first free ascent when it finally came yesterday surprise you?**

Ondra explained that his crew was surprised, but he genuinely believed he could succeed quickly. From day one of the trip, something felt fundamentally different—either the route seemed altered or he felt like a different climber entirely. The project suddenly became much more realistic.

**Q: You knew you could do all those boulder cruxes, but weren't sure how much the initial 20 meters of 8b would add to the difficulties?**

On the third day, Ondra made his first attempt from the ground and realized success was possible. Though he wasn't expecting the redpoint the next day due to fatigue and warm conditions, the extreme dryness ultimately didn't matter.

**Q: When did the send come?**

The redpoint occurred on his first real attempt of that day. Ondra emphasized he couldn't manage two solid attempts daily due to sore legs and calf muscles from the numerous knee-bars.

**Q: Tell us more about that specific leg training.**

The route features approximately seven knee-bar positions where he hangs upside down. This position allows nearly complete body relaxation except for the calf muscle, which endures intensive strain for several minutes. Ondra performed specialized exercises to extend knee-bar duration and improve arm recovery.

**Q: You also worked on your flexibility, too?**

With physiotherapist Klaus Isele, Ondra focused beyond just calf strength, including side abdominals and the ability to invert and execute high footjams. They addressed flexibility requirements for the difficult drop-knee movement in the crux.

**Q: You mentioned that straight away this trip you noticed that things were completely different.**

Following successful final days of his previous trip, Ondra trained intelligently at home. He completely excluded power-endurance work, never exceeding 15 consecutive moves. Instead, he concentrated on raw power through campus boarding and bouldering on steep terrain with slopers, underclings, and cracks—avoiding crimps entirely.

**Q: No endurance? For a 45 meter route?**

Ondra performed "fitness training" using interval sessions with multiple boulder problems followed by brief rest periods. This approach matched the project's specific demands perfectly. He believes excluding traditional endurance training contributed significantly to feeling considerably more powerful both at home and upon arriving at the crag.

**Q: Did that put you under pressure?**

Not initially. Pressure would have mounted had the ascent taken longer, introducing worries about route conditions and physical fatigue.

**Q: Then suddenly the redpoint...**

Ondra didn't anticipate a redpoint day, which proved beneficial psychologically. At the final knee-bar before crux one, he expected transitioning to "fight mode," but remained relaxed instead—achieving extraordinary precision on every movement. This relaxation prevented premature exhaustion.

**Q: And then?**

A toe-jam footlock preceded a massive drop-knee. When the toe-jam adhered exceptionally well, Ondra suddenly recognized his position and understood the significance of maintaining momentum.

**Q: Ahead of you was crux #2, an 8B sequence?**

A knee-bar preceded this section, where Ondra rested three to five minutes. Recognizing he wasn't physically exhausted, he felt genuine chances of completing the route, though he feared both the remaining cruxes. Ultimately, everything synchronized successfully.

**Q: So did you clip the chains with some energy to spare?**

Following crux three, a massive jug required only five 6C moves. Though Ondra anticipated complete relaxation upon reaching it, his legs felt numbingly exhausted from maximizing effort at the previous knee-bar. He composed himself seriously, fearing numb-leg mistakes on the final reach, rested briefly, then finished.

**Q: You mentioned in the past this is a possible 9c?**

Ondra stated: "I have the courage to say that this is the world's first 9c." He felt it substantially exceeded any 9b+ he'd completed. While acknowledging uncertainty about whether harder 9b+ climbs might qualify as 9c, he genuinely believes this represents 9c considering all contributing elements.

**Q: What happened when you finally clipped the anchor?**

Contrary to expectations, Ondra didn't produce his trademark scream. Instead, silence prevailed—an unprecedented experience. As emotions threatened overwhelming him, they somehow became trapped, leaving only tears swelling in his eyes.

**Q: So what does this climb represent to you?**

Ondra described this as definitely his most important climbing achievement, representing his greatest effort and deepest perseverance. Now complete, the experience feels incredibly rare and intensely meaningful.

Adam Ondra completed the first ascent of Silence (initially called "Project Hard"), graded 9c/5.15d, in Flatanger, Norway. This represents the hardest sport climbing route in the world.

**Route Details**

The 45-meter route features relentless overhanging terrain at 60 degrees. Ondra describes it as comprised of "hard boulder problems" requiring specific fitness. The route contains multiple cruxes:

- Crux 1: Ten moves of V15 difficulty—"possibly the hardest boulder problem" Ondra had completed
- Crux 2: Five moves of V13
- Crux 3: Approximately V10 difficulty
- Strategic kneebars providing crucial rest positions

**Training and Preparation**

Ondra invested approximately 60-70 days across seven trips spanning 2016-2017. His preparation included:

Specialized Training with Klaus (Physical Therapist):
- Calf strengthening through interval training enabled him to triple his kneebar hanging duration
- Side abdominal work for controlled foot placement
- Shoulder stabilization exercises

Training Structure:
- Six days weekly during intensive periods
- Campus board work, bouldering circuits, and endurance training
- 40-minute focused sessions rather than extended gym sessions
- Interval bouldering with short rest periods

**Why "Silence"?**

Ondra named the route after his psychological experience during the critical crux. Rather than screaming through the intense moves as he typically does, he achieved a state of complete focus and relaxation he describes as "silence"—a mental clarity that allowed precise execution without muscular tension.

**Key Insights**

On the Grade: Ondra believes 9c is accurate, noting the route demands considerably more investment than his three previous 9b+ ascents. However, he emphasizes uncertainty, acknowledging that alternative beta could alter difficulty assessment.

Physical Adaptations: His climbing style prioritizes efficiency through momentum and precision rather than raw power. He climbs quickly but deliberately, managing fatigue through movement quality.

Mental Approach: Visualization techniques and optimistic psychology enabled pressure management during the 20-minute sending attempt.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

On October 4, 2012, Adam Ondra achieved the first ascent of "The Change" in Hanshelleren cave at Flatanger, Norway, establishing what he graded as 9b+—at that time the world's hardest sport climbing route.

**Route Details**

The 55-meter route divides into two pitches. The opening section spans 9a+/b across 20 meters, featuring bouldery terrain with moves Ondra described as "some of the most crazy moves I have ever done." A poor kneebar provides a brief respite before easier climbing to the anchor. The second pitch, graded 9a, stretches 25 meters with demanding power-endurance sequences, compression moves, and relentless pumping interspersed with occasional jug rests.

**The Ascent Process**

Ondra invested five weeks across two climbing sessions—three weeks in summer and two in autumn—before success. Weather and moisture proved formidable obstacles; he noted, "I have never got that high before sending it, but during the send I realized that I would definitely fall off if got in the previous conditions."

**Ondra's Perspective**

Reflecting on the accomplishment, Ondra expressed profound satisfaction: "I can't believe it...It is definitely the most rewarding feeling I have ever had after sending the route." He emphasized that the route matched his climbing style perfectly and required extensive mental fortitude alongside physical capability.
