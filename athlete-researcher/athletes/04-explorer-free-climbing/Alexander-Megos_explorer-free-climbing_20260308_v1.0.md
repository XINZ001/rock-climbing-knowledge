```text
Published May 10, 2018 10:45PM

Alex Megos on Perfecto Mundo (5.15c), a sustained, 25-meter, 45-degree limestone route in northeast Spain.Ken Etzel

Yesterday, on May 9, Alex Megos stuck the crux on _Perfecto Mundo_—a dyno from a mono pocket to a big pinch that must be latched just right—then continued, pumped, towards the top. He’d visualized every move, every hold, and imagined how relaxed and composed he would feel taking it to the chains. Now his body wasn’t responding the way he’d planned: the pain was building in his forearms and everything felt harder, more tenuous than before. He eked out short rests whenever he could, but mainly he fought to hold on. He was nervous. With the hardest climbing behind him, he still had to make it through continuous 8b+ and 8c climbing (5.14) to reach a “tiny” boulder p­­roblem at the lip, followed by the final slab. Once past the boulder problem, he spent 10 minutes crawling up the slab, “trying to calm myself down,” stopping whenever possible to shake out. When he dropped the rope into the chains, he marked his first 5.15c and ticked the first ascent of the route.

Megos is now the second person, behind Adam Ondra, to author 5.15c (_La Dura Dura, Change, Vasil Vasil_) and the third person in the world to climb the grade, following Chris Sharma.

*   [Related: Video—Alex Megos Makes the First Ascent of _Fight Club_ (5.15b)](https://www.climbing.com/videos/alex-megos-makes-the-first-ascent-of-fight-club-5-15b/)

“Not only did I dream about climbing the route, but when I couldn’t fall asleep I’d visualize it in my head,” he told _Climbing_. “I always imagined climbing it without getting pumped.”

Chris Sharma bolted _Perfecto Mundo (_ which translates to “Perfect World”)nine years ago but, despite hard work on the route, has yet to redpoint it. When he got word that Megos and Stefano Ghisolfi were working the line, Sharma renewed his interest and drove out from his home a few times a week to exchange burns with the pair. Yesterday, Ghisolfi made it to Sharma’s previous highpoint, but ran out of time and flew back to Italy.

We caught up with Megos, 24, on May 10, after he’d returned from Racó de la Finestra, in the Province of Tarragona, northeast Spain, where his crew took follow-up shots of him on _Perfecto Mundo_.

How did you prepare for the route?
----------------------------------

Alex Megos: I didn’t specifically prepare it. Before coming to Finestra, I was climbing around Vegas for three weeks trying to get fit again as I was getting over a finger injury. While there I went to the Virgin River Gorge and did [_Necessary Evil_ (5.14c)](https://www.climbing.com/videos/michaela-kiersch-makes-the-first-female-ascent-of-necessary-evil-5-14c/) second try. I gave it a flash try but fell at the second hard move at the second crux. After that I had four days to get ready for a Bouldering IFSC World Cup—I failed to pass qualifiers—and from there I drove straight down to Spain to try this thing.

*   [Related:Watch Chris Sharma Project _Perfecto Mundo_ (Possible 5.15c)](https://www.climbing.com/videos/watch-chris-sharma-project-perfecto-mundo-possible-515c/)

What’s Finestra like?
---------------------

The whole crag is built like an amphitheater. It’s 45-degrees steep. You’re sitting under this wall and it feels quite epic when clouds roll in and there is thunder and lightning. We had a few days like that. There were always birds there.

Megos climbs hard while photographer Ken Etzel hangs nearby.Stefano Ghisolfi

Why this route?
---------------

I’ve come here quite a few times in the past few years. I always wanted to try it out. Then Stefano, after he tried it for a few days, said I should try it. He’s been here since April. Chris Sharma showed up a few weeks ago. It’s always more fun to work on a project with someone. Chris has it pretty dialed and definitely can do it; he just has to keep coming out and getting on it.

Chris was trying it back in the day and got close but then got distracted by _[La Dura Dura](https://www.climbing.com/videos/la-dura-complete-the-full-story-of-the-hardest-rock-climb-in-the-world/)_ while Adam Ondra was working on that line. It just didn’t get that much attention over the years. Not many people can climb that grade. It got forgotten about.

Can you describe the climbing on the 25-meter line?
---------------------------------------------------

It’s an amazing route. It’s 25 moves of 5.14c to reach a poor rest and then comes 10 moves to reach the crux: a jump move from the mono to the big pinch. Last week I stuck the move but got the pinch wrong. From then on, I knew I could do the route. I stuck the pinch four to five times without being able to move afterword. Once I got it in such a weird way I couldn’t move at all, so I started talking to Ken Etzel, the photographer next to me, about how my fingers were all fucked up on the hold and that I couldn’t move. I had enough power to hold on and talk for ages. Three or four days later I finally stuck the pinch move _and_ could do the next moves. From there it’s endurance 5.14 with few rests. The rock is yellow at the bottom and then goes to white and at the top it is gray. The whole thing is 45-degrees overhanging with a boulder problem to pull over into the slab. The final slab is easy 5.11, but you still have to be focused.

_Watch Megos project the line:_

Tell us about your climbing schedule for the route.
---------------------------------------------------

I was two days on, one day off, very consistent. During the last week, on day one, I would only give it tries to see how far I would get. On the second day, I would give it one to three burns and then afterward work on the sections and get them dialed, and get the slab wired. I would always see the first day as a redpoint day and day two was a split day.

What’s next? Is _Silence_ (5.15d) on your radar?
------------------------------------------------

I don’t think Flatanger [in Norway] is my style. I think I would have a hard time climbing something there at my limit… I don’t really know [what’s next]: driving back home, enjoying a few barbecues. In July I’m competing in four lead climbing World Cups.

*   [Related:Interview—Adam Ondra On What It Took to Climb the World’s First 5.15d](https://www.climbing.com/videos/la-dura-complete-the-full-story-of-the-hardest-rock-climb-in-the-world/)

Anything else you’d like to add?
--------------------------------

I want to thank Chris and Stefano for encouraging me to try the route and the good times we had at the crag. Obviously, Chris has always been a big inspiration for me and it’s great climbing with him.I competed in my first youth World Cup with Stefan 10 years ago, and we’ve stayed in contact ever since.
```

Alexander Megos (19) onsight Estado Critico 9a
==============================================

[Desnivel](http://desnivel.com/escalada-roca/alex-megos-consigue-en-siurana-el-primer-9a-a-vista-de-la-historia) reports that Alexander Megos has potentially made the first 9a onsight in the history of climbing by Estado Critico in Siurana. He also did another 134 routes and boulders 8a and harder. He has previously redpointed a couple 9a's and onsighted some 8b+'s. In 2009 and 2010, Alexander Megos won eight straight European Youth Cups. It should be noted that Adam Ondra in 2012 onsighted three 9a's, out of which two were well confirmed, for which he gave a personal grade of 8c+. (c) Ignacio Sandoval Buron

Alexander Megos claims second 9a onsight with TCT at Gravere
============================================================

Interview with Alexander Megos who on Sunday, May 21, 2017 at Gravere in Italy managed to onsight the 9a sport climb TCT. This is the German’s second 9a onsight after the historic world’s first 9a onsight in 2013.

_Last Sunday at Gravere in Val di Susa, NW Italy, [Alexander Megos](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news?search\_opt=checked&parola=Alexander%20Megos) managed to onsight [TCT](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news/climbing/stefano-ghisolfi-frees-tct-9a-at-gravere.html), the climb freed in 2014 by Stefano Ghisolfi and dedicated to his young friend [Tito Claudio Traversa](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news/climbing/goodbye-tito-traversa.html). This is Megos’ second 9a in the purest of styles after his amazing ascent of [Estado Critico at Siurana in Spain in March 2013](http://www.planetmountain.com/en/news/interviews/alexander-megos-interview-after-worlds-first-9a-onsight.html), thanks to which at the time the 19-year-old German climber set the climbing world alight the historic first 9a onsight. It’s worth remembering that in addition to Megos, the only other person who has managed to onsight 9a is Adam Ondra (he currently has 3 to his name), and that 9a onsight is currently considered the absolute limit in sports climbing._

**Four years have passed since your onsight of _Estado Critico_. Did your insight of TCT come as a surprise?**

 Yes, actually a bit more than 4 years have passed since I onsighted _Estado Critico_. I was really surprised. As I warmed up I felt really bad, I rushed things, didn’t find the right flow and even felt my injured finger a bit. After warming up on a 6c I then climbed an 8a and started wondering whether I should try the 9a, or save it up for another day. It was Claudia Ghisolfi who persuaded me to try the route. And then I thought, why not? It’s always worth giving it a go.

**Until recently you’ve always believed you’d never manage to onsight 9a again.**

 I’d never felt like I was climbing good enough to have 9a onsight within reach. Yeah, I’m obviously I better climber now and far stronger, but nevertheless I never had the impression that I was strong enough.

**Well you proved yourself wrong! We’re sure you know that _TCT_ is a somewhat special route in Italy**

 Yes, I know that _TCT_ is an homage to Tito. Unfortunately I never met him, but I knew him from the news and was really shocked to hear about his death.

**Four years ago in Siurana you climbed without any external pressure. You weren’t as famous as you are today and at the time no one expected a performance of this sort**

 Yes, I had no pressure back then. Nowadays things are a bit different, of course, but I try to put myself under as little pressure as possible. On Sunday for instance I had no pressure at all. I felt so weak that I really didn’t expect anything and so when I climbed I was fairly relaxed.

**So how did things go?**

 The ascent took about 10 minutes I think. The first part went relatively quickly and ran smoothly, then I got to a rest before the last section. This last part is approximately 8 meters long, 45 degrees overhanging, with small and distant crimps. So pretty much exactly my style of climbing. When I reached the lip of the roof I couldn’t believe what I’d just done. But then I needed circa 2 minutes to clip the chain as I didn’t see a hold.

**How close were you to your limit?**

 I was pretty pumped and spent when I got to the top, but I wasn’t at my absolute limit. What amazed me though was that I felt exhausted for the rest of the day. Although I climbed an 8c and an 8c+, I felt completely spent after the onsight.

**How often had you tried to onsight 9a prior to this second success?**

 I must confess I hadn’t attempted many onsights. Acutally, I think that was the first 9a I really tried to onsight. Onisghting a route of these difficulties is not always easy. In the Frankenjura for example, where usually climb, this is very difficult indeed. And if you’re somewhere else then you first have to find a suitable 9a, one which ideally has the quickdraw in it already, then you have to feel good and be strong mentally. Until now these factors had never really combined properly.

**Now that you’ve confirmed your 9a onsight: what’s the next step?**

 Well the next step would be to onsight some more 9a’s. And then obviously onsight a 9a+. Time will tell.

### 原文全文（英文连贯版）

**Alexander, tell us how this onsight came about?**

"In truth my plan was to try La Rambla, but since I didn't know the line precisely and I didn't have a guidebook, I decided to give Estado Critico a go. I didn't really plan to on-sight it. I simply wanted to see how high I could climb. And luckily I managed to reach the top."

**So what can you tell us about your ascent?**

"Estado Critico shares the same start as Kalea Borroka (8b+) up an overhanging crack. I almost fell there but somehow managed to reach a good rest. After Estado Critico branches off from Kalea Borroka the climbing suits my style perfectly. Overhanging, up small crimps and no large moves."

**Were you ever worried that you might not succeed?**

"I only started getting nervous at the last bolt below the chain, when I realised that I could actually make it."

**What did you know about the route?**

"Before climbing it I only knew that my friend Felix Neumärker had repeated it. I didn't know who else had climbed it, I didn't know who had done the first ascent and I knew nothing about a broken hold. So I didn't really know anything apart from where I needed to climb."

**You didn't know anything, but anyone interested can find loads of videos online... What's your view about this?**

"Videos can certainly be a problem if you want to try to on-sight something. But if you want to try it, then you shouldn't watch videos beforehand. It's as simple as that. Naturally I'd never climbed Kalea Borroka which shares the same start as Estado Critico beforehand and I hadn't watched any videos, otherwise I couldn't say that mine was on-sight. It's really important to be honest about these things."

**Any thoughts about the grade?**

"I cannot and do not want to say anything about the grade. During my on-sight everything ran perfectly and nevertheless it was really difficult. I don't feel I can say anything about the grade."

**When did you realise what you had just achieved?**

"I only fully comprehended what I had just done when I was back on the ground, when someone asked me if I had just climbed that 9a onsight and I answered yes. Slowly but surely it dawned on me and I realised what I had done."

**So what can you tell us about other things you've done?**

"In the past my best on-sight was 8b+. I'd sent five 8b+ onsight, two 8c flash and one 8c+ flash (Pure Imagination)."

**But with results like this, why have we heard so little about you in the past?**

"I reckon it might be because normally I don't publish on the internet what I've just climbed. Usually this is posted by others who have seen me at the crag. Immediately publishing something online really isn't that important for me. It's not my style."

**Where will we see you in the future?**

"I don't have any concrete plans and really don't want to make any. I'll just let myself be surprised by what the day brings, because those who plan the night before, plan twice. Having said that, I'll obviously try to on-sight and redpoint some hard classics here in Siurana, but I haven't got any specific routes in mind. I'll just decide on the spur of the moment, depending on what looks good."

### 摘要 / 备注

> 2013年 Siurana Estado Critico 9a Onsight 当日即时采访。覆盖：onsight 心态（偶然、无准备）、视频观看对 onsight 纯粹性的影响、不关注媒体曝光的风格。核心话题：首登、心理、onsight 哲学。

Alexander Megos is one of the world's most successful sport climbers who competes regularly in World Cup events and has represented Germany at the Olympics twice. The 32-year-old from Erlangen published his autobiography "Frei am Fels" (Free on the Rock), discussing setbacks, eating disorders, and overcoming performance pressure.

**Lacrux: "Free on the Rock" begins very personally. Were there also topics that you found difficult to write about?**

Alex Megos: Yes, absolutely. Talking about personal issues, especially the deep ones, is always difficult. Topics like eating disorders are still often taboo. Even in climbing, it's not easy to openly admit failure. Most of the time, you only hear about successes, not the processes behind them.

What I hope is that readers understand climbing isn't just a sport, but also a philosophy of life. Life is full of ups and downs—things can't always go well. We usually hear about athletes when they're doing well, but rarely when they're struggling. It was important to me to show that people aren't alone with their problems.

**When would you say that climbing became part of your identity?**

I started climbing young as a hobby alongside other sports. At twelve or thirteen, I realized how important it was to me. Around fifteen or sixteen, I defined myself largely through climbing and performance. My entire environment revolved around it—my friends, vacations, everything. Climbing was no longer just a sport.

**Especially with children who are strong from an early age, one often wonders: Is that their own intrinsic motivation or does it come from their parents pushing them? What was it like for you?**

My parents didn't push me at all. In my youth, they were more like brakes. The drive for achievement always came from within myself. I put that expectation on myself entirely by my own volition because it was simply important to me. Alongside the pure joy of climbing, competitive drive was added very early on. I've always had it, even as a child. Climbing came extremely well to me, so I considered the performance aspect as well. This drive has always been part of my identity—not just in climbing.

**Would you say that the fun of climbing was sometimes less there because the pressure was so high?**

There were definitely times when I found it difficult to cope with the pressure. I put so much pressure on myself that climbing was sometimes less enjoyable. But for me, that was a sign that I needed to learn how to manage this pressure and change my mindset. Today, I really enjoy feeling that pressure—the thrill of whether certain projects will work out.

I think it was simply a learning process that probably everyone goes through to some extent. For me, that was especially true in my younger years—particularly when I started earning my living from climbing. It took several years to learn.

**What helped you get out of that situation?**

The realization that "you don't save lives while climbing—or really, in anything you do." No one dies, and no one is saved simply because you complete a particular route or not. In the end, it's "just" about climbing. You have to keep reminding yourself of that.

**Looking back on those times today, what do you think about them?**

I believe that all my highs and lows have been important for my personality and development. Just when I started to see myself as a professional, I stopped taking rest days. In my mind, climbing was my job, and on rest days I often felt useless. I found it difficult to endure them, even though they're extremely important for the sport and recovery.

Back then, I didn't have many other projects in my life. I was sitting at home thinking: I have nothing to do, I can't just sit around and do nothing.

Frequent injuries made me realize I needed to change my training—that it's not just what you do while climbing, but also what you do during rest time.

**You dedicate an entire chapter to the route "Estado Critico" (9a). This route brought you into the media spotlight. What did that ascent mean to you back then?**

For my career, that was obviously an incredible kickstarter. I think that even without "Estado Critico" I could have become a professional climber, but this massively accelerated the process. Especially because I became known everywhere overnight and didn't have to build that recognition over months or years.

**Did you believe you would become a professional immediately afterwards?**

When I climbed "Estado Critico," I was still just a hobby climber in my own eyes. I was ambitious and a good climber, but after school, I was essentially taking a gap year. I assumed that after a break, I would eventually start university and have a regular job. Making money from climbing was something I couldn't imagine back then.

**In your book, you primarily address a topic that accompanied you on your path to becoming a professional. Why was it important for you to address the topic of anorexia so openly?**

I simply see that the former IFSC—now World Climbing—isn't doing anything about underweight athletes who obviously have a problem and are still allowed to compete. That's why I thought: I'll take matters into my own hands. It simply has to be addressed. It was important because there are many people in climbing struggling with this issue.

You only have to look around at the World Cup: if everyone who follows World Cups can immediately name two or three people who obviously have a problem, then those in charge are either not interested or not looking closely enough.

I hope that many young athletes understand that it's a real problem with long-term consequences if not addressed. Some people who lose weight think, "Ah, I'm just more disciplined than everyone else." I used to think that too. You believe you're tougher, more extreme, more professional—just because you're losing weight. But anyone can lose weight. It has nothing to do with being better than others. It only shows that you haven't understood how to truly be a professional. Coercion has nothing to do with discipline.

**How has your understanding of discipline or self-discipline changed over the years?**

I've realized that compulsion or the feeling of having to do something has nothing to do with discipline. I used to think discipline meant I had to do pull-ups every day and train for many hours—simply because I "had to" do it. It took me a really long time to understand that discipline actually means being able to decide what makes sense and then doing it, even if it's not fun. It also means letting go of things that don't make sense, even if they would sometimes be more enjoyable.

**What advice would you give to young athletes today when they prioritize performance over their own health?**

I was in that situation myself. The crucial message is: You can't perform at your best in the long run in this state—even if it works in the short term. Anyone who truly wants to call themselves a professional athlete has to think long-term, to the point where they can make a living from climbing for five, ten, or ideally twenty years. You don't achieve that by performing at your peak for two years and then disappearing into obscurity for five. It only works by training hard, paying attention to your body, and being able to perform consistently over the long term.

I have found that in the long run, you are significantly more efficient if you manage to maintain a reasonable weight.

**You have spoken very openly in favor of banning athletes with a BMI that is too low. Can you elaborate on your reasoning?**

It's certainly the simplest and quickest solution, without any fuss. It's easy to measure and simple to implement. I think it's perfectly fine to ban people for health reasons if they're too thin. Then it's just a case of: "Gain some weight." Of course, if someone really has a problem, that doesn't help. But the fact is: most athletes with a BMI that is too low are not healthy. If someone calls themselves a professional athlete, they can also take on a little responsibility.

**Would you say that this competitive machine contains mechanisms that promote unhealthy developments rather than prevent them?**

Of course! The mere fact that you're not banned for being visibly underweight is already problematic. Those very people who then lose weight sometimes improve their ranking by twenty places from one year to the next—clearly because they've lost five or ten kilos. This then reinforces the misconception: "I'm on the right track."

**Would you say that mental support in climbing now has the same importance as strength or technique training?**

In competition, it becomes clear that mental performance is often at least as important as physical performance. There are people who appear "weak" but can achieve incredible things in competition because they can mentally focus perfectly in that moment. The support from the national team coaches is important for me, but working weekly with my mental coach or my personal trainers is far more crucial. That's the immediate environment that truly supports me. Therefore, I believe that mental support is at least as important as training support.

**Keyword: Mental coach: What has this training changed for you?**

On the one hand, I can better focus my performance, and on the other hand, I've learned how to make everyday life and training more efficient—including recovery. A lot of it has to do with breathing, but it also involves many things that happen outside of climbing. For example, I've learned how to deal with everyday stress and what I can do to minimize stressors. We've worked intensively on these topics. Today, mental strength means discipline to me. I would have said the same thing ten or even fifteen years ago—only back then I confused discipline with coercion.

**What about future plans? Is the focus more on rock climbing or on the competition stage?**

My focus is clearly on rock climbing, especially for the next year. I don't want to rule out continuing to compete, and I don't rule out that it might become relevant again at some point. But right now, I just want things to go well outdoors on the rock. Competition climbing is temporary, rock climbing is forever. I still have too many projects outdoors that are important to me, and that's why I'll be investing the majority of my time there.
